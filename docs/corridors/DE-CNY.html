German bank transfers to China carry a total cost of approximately EUR 300–700 on EUR 10,000, comprising an FX markup of approximately 1.5–5% above the ECB EUR/CNY reference rate, a percentage-based transfer fee of 1.5% of the transfer amount, and SWIFT correspondent deductions. Deutsche Bank charges 1.5% of the amount in transfer fees alone — a percentage fee, not a flat charge — making large transfers disproportionately costly at that institution.

Fintech providers occupy a substantially lower cost tier. Airwallex at approximately 0.6% total represents the current lower bound for the Germany–China corridor on a B2B basis. WorldFirst at approximately 1.15% (0.75% FX ceiling plus 0.40% China payment fee) offers the next tier, with broader eligibility and 1688.com payment capability. Wise applies 0% markup plus a transparent fee equivalent to approximately 1.5% on EUR 10,000.

Frankfurt hosts a designated RMB clearing center (Bank of China Frankfurt), backed by a EUR 45B/CNY 350B ECB–PBOC currency swap renewed September 2025. Despite this infrastructure advantage, German retail and SME bank customers receive no benefit from clearing center pricing; markup is applied at standard retail rates.

§3 — Key Findings

1
China reclaimed the position of Germany's number one trading partner in 2025 (EUR 251.8B bilateral). German banks charge an estimated 3–7% total cost for transfers that fintechs complete at 0.6–1.5%, representing the widest bank-to-fintech differential observed across all EEA corridors researched to date.
2
EUR/CNY carries a 17.9% weight in China's CFETS basket — 6.6× the weight of GBP (2.7%). This structural liquidity advantage implies tighter wholesale spreads on EUR/CNY, yet German bank retail markups of 1.5–5% exceed those observed at most UK banks. The clearing infrastructure advantage is not passed through to retail or SME customers.
3
Frankfurt hosts a designated RMB clearing center (Bank of China Frankfurt) backed by a EUR 45B/CNY 350B ECB–PBOC swap, renewed September 2025. This positions Germany as the primary EUR/CNY hub in continental Europe. It does not translate to lower retail transfer costs.
4
Deutsche Bank's transfer fee is 1.5% of the transfer amount — a percentage fee, not a flat fee — making it one of the most expensive institutions for large transfers across all corridors researched. On EUR 100,000, Deutsche Bank's transfer fee alone is an estimated EUR 1,500, before any FX markup is applied.
5
Commerzbank's standard SWIFT transfer takes 5–7 business days — among the slowest observed across any bank in any corridor in this research series. Combined with a transfer fee of approximately 1.5% of amount plus EUR 22.50, Commerzbank represents the slowest and one of the more expensive standard options.
6
HypoVereinsbank (UniCredit subsidiary) carries the highest estimated average FX markup of the five German banks surveyed, at approximately 2.9% above the ECB reference rate. This estimate is sourced from third-party comparison data and carries medium confidence pending published rate confirmation.
7
Airwallex at approximately 0.6% total (0.5% FX markup plus 0.1% CNY payout fee) is the lowest-cost provider on this corridor for B2B transfers. Its NL Central Bank EU license covers EEA operations. Eligibility for local CNY delivery requires the recipient to be a registered mainland China entity.
8
Wise's EUR→CNY fee of approximately 1.5% is notably higher than its GBP→CNY fee of approximately 0.91% on a comparable transfer. CNY delivery from EUR carries a structurally higher Wise fee. This corridor-specific difference is not disclosed prominently in Wise's marketing materials.
9
Germany imposes zero capital controls on outbound payments under Article 63 TFEU. The only reporting requirement is statistical: transfers exceeding EUR 50,000 must be reported to the Bundesbank under the AWV (Aussenwirtschaftsverordnung). The threshold was raised from EUR 12,500 in January 2025. This is a statistical obligation, not a restriction on transfer execution.

Layer 3 — FX Risk Buffer Embedded in USD Prices (Buyer Pays, Indirectly)

Chinese suppliers embed an estimated 2–5% FX risk buffer in USD-quoted prices to protect against EUR/USD fluctuation between invoice date and settlement. German buyers paying in USD absorb this cost structurally within the unit price. Switching to CNY payment eliminates the rationale for this buffer and typically produces reductions of 1–3% in effective unit prices.

The EUR→CNY direct route accounts for approximately 25–30% of Germany–China payment flow. The buyer converts EUR to CNY and the payment is delivered via CIPS or local Chinese rails. Frankfurt's RMB clearing infrastructure should in principle support competitive EUR/CNY rates; in practice, retail and SME rates remain at standard markup levels.

§8 — Decision Framework

Scenario A
SME importer paying Chinese suppliers monthly
WorldFirst (approximately 1.15% total) or Airwallex (approximately 0.6%, B2B only) represent the lowest-cost options. WorldFirst's 1688.com World Pay capability (0.80%) is a differentiating feature for buyers sourcing through that platform. Airwallex is lower cost but requires the recipient to meet Chinese entity eligibility requirements.
Scenario B
Large corporate with high-volume China payments
Direct negotiation with Deutsche Bank or Commerzbank trade finance desks may produce rates below published retail markups. Alternatively, Airwallex or OFX with volume pricing offer competitive rates. OFX offers forward contracts, relevant for buyers hedging EUR/CNY exposure on future purchase orders.
Scenario C
1688.com or Chinese marketplace buyer
WorldFirst World Pay (0.80% for 1688.com payments) is the only provider in this survey with a specific marketplace payment product for Chinese domestic platforms. Standard SWIFT or fintech routes cannot directly pay 1688.com seller accounts.
Scenario D
Personal or small-value transfer
Wise or N26 (Wise-powered) are the most appropriate options for personal transfers or infrequent small transfers below EUR 1,000. Remitly offers Alipay and WeChat delivery but is restricted to personal use and carries a higher effective rate.
Scenario E
Forward contract required
OFX offers forward contracts for EUR/CNY, allowing buyers to lock in a rate for future payments. Deutsche Bank's corporate trade desk also offers FX forwards. No other fintech provider in this survey offers forward contracts as a standard product.

§9 — Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to send EUR 10,000 from Germany to China?
Through a standard German bank, the estimated total cost is approximately EUR 300–700, comprising an FX markup of approximately 1.5–5% above the ECB EUR/CNY reference rate (1 EUR = 7.95 CNY as of 3 April 2026), a transfer fee of approximately 1.5% of the amount, and SWIFT correspondent deductions. Through WorldFirst, the estimated cost is approximately EUR 115, based on a 0.75% maximum FX margin and a 0.40% China payment fee. Through Airwallex (B2B), the estimated cost is approximately EUR 60 based on 0.6% total.
Is Deutsche Bank more expensive than other German banks for transfers to China?
Deutsche Bank charges a transfer fee of 1.5% of the transfer amount — a percentage fee rather than a flat fee. On EUR 100,000, the transfer fee alone is EUR 1,500 before any FX markup is applied. This structure makes Deutsche Bank disproportionately expensive for large transfers compared to banks with flat-fee structures. At EUR 10,000, Deutsche Bank's estimated total cost is approximately EUR 400–600. Published fee schedule: db.com/de/international-transfers (data collected April 2026).
What is the Frankfurt RMB clearing center and does it reduce transfer costs?
Frankfurt hosts a designated RMB clearing center operated by Bank of China Frankfurt, supported by a EUR 45B/CNY 350B currency swap between the ECB and PBOC, renewed September 2025. This positions Frankfurt as the primary EUR/CNY clearing hub in continental Europe. In practice, the institutional cost advantage of the clearing infrastructure is not passed through to retail or SME customers, who transact at standard published markup rates. The clearing center primarily benefits interbank EUR/CNY settlement.
Does N26 offer cheaper transfers to China than Deutsche Bank?
Yes, substantially. N26 uses Wise's transfer infrastructure for international payments, producing an effective rate of approximately 1.5% on EUR 10,000 EUR→CNY via Alipay. Deutsche Bank's estimated total cost for the same transfer is approximately EUR 400–600. N26 is BaFin-licensed and headquartered in Berlin. For business customers with regular China payment flows, a dedicated WorldFirst or Wise business account typically produces lower per-transaction cost than N26's consumer-oriented product.
What is the AWV reporting threshold for transfers to China?
Under the Aussenwirtschaftsverordnung (AWV), transfers exceeding EUR 50,000 must be reported to the Deutsche Bundesbank. This threshold was raised from EUR 12,500 in January 2025. The obligation is statistical and administrative in nature; it is not a restriction on transfer execution. Germany imposes no capital controls on outbound payments under Article 63 TFEU. Reports are submitted electronically via the Bundesbank's Z-Meldungen system.
How long does a transfer from Germany to China take?
German bank SWIFT transfers typically take 3–7 business days, with Commerzbank's standard route taking 5–7 days — the slowest observed across all German institutions surveyed. Fintech options are substantially faster: Airwallex delivers same-day local CNY; WorldFirst delivers in 1–2 business days; Wise delivers 74% of transfers instantly or near-instantly. Cut-off times apply; transfers initiated after banking hours may be processed on the next business day.
What is the cheapest way to send EUR to China for business payments?
Airwallex at approximately 0.6% total (0.5% FX markup plus 0.1% CNY payout fee) represents the lowest observed cost for B2B transfers on the Germany–China corridor, subject to recipient entity eligibility requirements. WorldFirst at approximately 1.15% (0.75% FX ceiling plus 0.40% China payment fee) is the next tier and carries broader eligibility and 1688.com payment capability. Both options produce estimated savings of EUR 385–435 per EUR 10,000 transfer relative to a typical German bank.
Does EUR/CNY have a liquidity advantage over GBP/CNY?
EUR/CNY carries a 17.9% weight in China's CFETS reference basket, compared with 2.7% for GBP/CNY — a ratio of 6.6×. This weight reflects higher transaction volumes and in principle supports tighter wholesale spreads on EUR/CNY. However, retail and SME bank markups on the Germany–China corridor (1.5–5%) exceed those observed at most UK high-street banks (2.2–4%), suggesting the wholesale liquidity advantage is not systematically passed through to end customers. Fintech providers on both corridors converge at approximately 0.5–0.75%.

§10 — Corridor Context

Settlement Currency Composition

Germany–China trade payments use three primary currency channels. USD routing (approximately 50–55%) remains dominant, particularly for commodity and manufacturing supply chain payments where USD is the global contract currency. EUR→CNY direct routing (approximately 25–30%) reflects the EUR/CNY CFETS weight and Frankfurt's clearing center infrastructure. CNY direct payments (approximately 15–20%) are growing as Chinese suppliers increasingly accept domestic CNY payment via Alipay, WeChat Pay, and CIPS channels.

Frankfurt RMB Infrastructure

Frankfurt hosts the designated RMB clearing center for the Eurozone, operated by Bank of China Frankfurt. The ECB–PBOC currency swap line (EUR 45B / CNY 350B), renewed September 2025, provides EUR/CNY liquidity for the clearing center and supports German corporate China trade flows at an institutional level. The CFETS 17.9% weight for EUR reflects this institutional infrastructure.

Trade Relationship

China reclaimed the position of Germany's number one trading partner in 2025 with EUR 251.8B bilateral trade volume. Germany recorded an estimated EUR 89B trade deficit with China. German exports to China are concentrated in machinery, automobiles, and chemicals. German imports from China include electronics, machinery, and consumer goods. The volume and structural nature of this trade relationship underpins sustained demand for EUR/CNY payment corridors at all value segments.

Regulatory Framework

Payment services in Germany are regulated by BaFin (Bundesanstalt für Finanzdienstleistungsaufsicht) and, for systemically important banks, by the ECB under the Single Supervisory Mechanism (SSM). International transfers from Germany operate under PSD2 (SEPA) for euro payments within the EEA, and SWIFT for non-EEA destinations including China. Germany imposes no capital controls on outbound payments; the only reporting obligation is statistical AWV notification for transfers exceeding EUR 50,000. The EUR 50,000 threshold was raised from EUR 12,500 effective January 2025.

§11 — Methodology & Limitations

What This Analysis Does Not Cover

  • Negotiated corporate rates. All data represents published retail and SME rates. Large-volume customers at Deutsche Bank, Commerzbank, or fintech providers may access rates not disclosed in published tariffs.
  • Forward contract pricing. OFX and Deutsche Bank corporate desk offer EUR/CNY forward contracts; specific forward pricing is not included.
  • Private banking and premium account rates. HypoVereinsbank UniCredit Premium and Deutsche Bank Private Bank rate structures are not captured.
  • DZ Bank / Volksbank and Sparkasse branch-level variation. Approximately 350 Sparkasse institutions and approximately 700 Volksbank branches set pricing independently. Figures represent estimated ranges only.
  • TransferGo EUR→CNY. TransferGo routes via USD on this corridor and is not competitive vs. direct CNY providers; excluded from comparison table.
  • HypoVereinsbank FX markup. Data sourced from third-party comparison platforms, not published tariff. Medium confidence only.
