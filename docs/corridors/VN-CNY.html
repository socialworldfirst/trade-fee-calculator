03 — Key Findings 8 findings • research grade
  • 01 Vietnam is a fintech desert for outbound payments. Wise explicitly states "You can only send VND from your Wise account to bank accounts in Vietnam." No major global fintech serves outbound from Vietnam. Airwallex requires a non-Vietnamese entity. This is a structural gap, not a temporary restriction — it reflects the licensing burden of SBV regulation combined with the complexity of VND liquidity.
  • 02 Vietnam and China share a 1,281 km land border with formal border trade payment infrastructure. Vietnam's Circular 17/2025 expanded direct CNY/VND settlement across 7 border provinces, but formal trade payments still route overwhelmingly through USD. Border CNY settlement is growing but represents a small fraction of total corridor flows.
  • 03 Vietnam is the only major ASEAN economy without a bilateral central bank currency swap with the PBOC. The PBOC has active swap lines with most ASEAN central banks (Bank Negara Malaysia, Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas, Bank Indonesia, Bank of Thailand) but not the State Bank of Vietnam. This absence limits institutional RMB liquidity available to Vietnamese banks and reinforces USD as the corridor's settlement currency.
  • 04 Double conversion (VND→USD→CNY) is the standard flow. Vietnamese banks apply 1.5–4% FX markup on the VND→USD leg, then Chinese banks add 0.3–1% on USD→CNY. Total cost is additive across two separate FX events. Direct VND→CNY conversion exists only at border bank branches and is not scalable for general commercial volumes.
  • 05 Vietnam-China bilateral trade reached $254 billion in 2024 (+14.6% YoY), on pace for $300B+ in 2026. Vietnam runs a structural deficit of ~$60–70B with China, creating massive net payment demand flowing from Vietnamese importers to Chinese exporters — squarely in WorldFirst's direction.
  • 06 SBV manages VND within a +/–5% band around the daily central reference rate. All FX transactions must route through SBV-licensed Vietnamese banks. Circular 20/2022 governs outbound current account payments, requiring documentation of underlying trade transactions. Private companies cannot independently hold or transfer foreign currency.
  • 07 Cross-border QR payment launched December 2025 (NAPAS + UnionPay + ICBC + Vietcombank) — retail and tourism use cases initially. Vietnamese tourists in China can pay at UnionPay merchants; Chinese tourists in Vietnam at NAPAS terminals. Does not currently cover B2B trade. Signals deepening payment infrastructure and a potential future pathway for small-value trade settlement.
  • 08 WorldFirst's 1688 World Pay (0.80% platform fee) is the standout feature for this corridor. Many Vietnamese importers source directly from 1688.com — Alibaba's domestic wholesale marketplace. No Vietnamese bank offers direct 1688 checkout integration. WorldFirst's total 1688 cost (~2.3%) is still cheaper than bank alternatives for most buyers, and the platform integration removes the need for a sourcing agent intermediary.

09 — Frequently Asked Questions 8 questions • Vietnam-specific
Why can't I use Wise to send money from Vietnam? +
Wise explicitly restricts outbound payments from Vietnam. Their published terms state: "You can only send VND from your Wise account to bank accounts in Vietnam." This means Wise can receive money into a Vietnamese account but cannot process outbound international transfers originating from VND. This is not a temporary restriction — it reflects Wise's strategic decision not to obtain outbound payment licensing from the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV). No equivalent global fintech holds this license.
What is CNY border trade settlement and how does it work? +
Vietnam's Circular 17/2025 expanded direct CNY/VND settlement across 7 border provinces (Lang Son, Cao Bang, Ha Giang, Lao Cai, Quang Ninh, Dien Bien, Lai Chau). Border traders can settle in local currencies through licensed bank branches in these provinces without converting to USD first. This reduces double-conversion cost but is only available for qualifying cross-border trade transactions, not general remittance or non-border commercial payments. Available volumes are small relative to total corridor flows.
Why is double conversion (VND to USD to CNY) the standard flow? +
Vietnam and China do not have a bilateral central bank currency swap agreement. The PBOC has swap lines with most ASEAN central banks — but not the State Bank of Vietnam. This absence means Vietnamese banks lack direct CNY liquidity priced in VND. The practical result: virtually all formal trade payments convert VND to USD first (where deep liquidity exists), then USD to CNY. Each conversion adds 0.5–2% in FX markup, so total corridor cost is additive across two separate FX events.
What does SBV regulation require for outbound payments? +
All foreign currency transactions in Vietnam must route through licensed Vietnamese banks regulated by the State Bank of Vietnam. The SBV manages VND within a +/–5% band around the daily central reference rate. Circular 20/2022 governs outbound current account payments, requiring documentation of the underlying trade transaction for business transfers. Private companies cannot independently hold or transfer foreign currency — all FX must route through a licensed bank intermediary. This is why global fintechs without SBV licensing cannot legally operate outbound payments from Vietnam.
What is the cheapest way to send money from Vietnam to China? +
WorldFirst is currently the cheapest documented option for business payments from Vietnam to China, at approximately 1.9% total cost (1.5% FX markup plus 0.40% China payment fee for high-volume clients). Vietnamese bank alternatives range from 1.7% (BIDV best case) to 4.5% (Vietcombank worst case). No global fintech — Wise, Airwallex, Revolut — serves outbound from Vietnam. For large enterprises, BIDV or VietinBank negotiated rates may be competitive with WorldFirst on FX, but WorldFirst's digital platform and same-day CNY settlement are differentiating advantages that justify the switch for most SMEs.
How does WorldFirst's 1688 World Pay work for Vietnam buyers? +
WorldFirst's 1688 World Pay enables direct checkout payments on 1688.com (Alibaba's domestic wholesale marketplace) from a WorldFirst account. The total cost is approximately 1.5% FX + 0.80% platform fee = 2.3%. This is uniquely valuable for Vietnamese importers sourcing from 1688, as no Vietnamese bank offers direct 1688 checkout integration. Without World Pay, buyers must use a sourcing agent (typically 5–10% commission) or wire payments via SWIFT to the supplier's bank account, which can take 2–5 business days and requires the supplier to manually match payments to orders.
How fast are payments from Vietnam to China? +
WorldFirst offers same-day CNY settlement for payments processed through CNAPS (China's domestic payment rail). Vietnamese banks via SWIFT typically take 2–5 business days, depending on correspondent banking relationships and compliance screening volumes. Border trade CNY/VND settlement under Circular 17/2025 can be same-day through local bank branches. Speed is a meaningful differentiator for Vietnamese importers with tight supplier payment terms — particularly when ordering via 1688 where supplier dispatch is conditional on payment confirmation.
What is the cross-border QR payment launched December 2025? +
In December 2025, NAPAS (Vietnam's national payment switch), UnionPay, ICBC, and Vietcombank jointly launched cross-border QR payment between Vietnam and China. As of April 2026, this covers retail and tourism use cases — Vietnamese tourists in China can pay at UnionPay merchants, and Chinese tourists in Vietnam can pay at NAPAS-enabled merchants. It does not currently cover B2B trade payments. It signals deepening payment infrastructure and is a likely future pathway for simplified small-value trade settlements, potentially reducing reliance on SWIFT for low-value orders.

Vietnam's position in this corridor is structurally constrained in ways that distinguish it from all other ASEAN corridors. The absence of a PBOC-SBV bilateral currency swap — unusual for a country of this trade volume with China — means institutional RMB liquidity is not available in Vietnam at scale. All commercial FX transactions route through the USD intermediary step, making double-conversion cost structural rather than incidental.

The SBV's regulatory framework (Circular 20/2022 for outbound, Circular 17/2025 for border CNY expansion) is actively modernizing, but the licensing barrier for foreign fintechs remains high. The December 2025 cross-border QR launch is the first material fintech-adjacent infrastructure development in the corridor and may eventually reduce SWIFT dependency for small-value trade.

Vietnam's structural trade deficit with China — importing far more than it exports — means the payment demand direction strongly favors outbound VND/USD → CNY flows. This creates a concentrated demand pool that WorldFirst's VN tier pricing directly addresses.

11 — Data Limitations Methodology notes • Next review: October 2026
  • Bank FX markups are estimated ranges based on published indicative rates and market intelligence. Vietnamese banks do not publicly disclose exact FX margin schedules — actual rates are negotiated per client relationship and transaction size.
  • No fintech comparison is possible for outbound from Vietnam. The "fintech route" row reflects confirmed product restrictions, not estimated costs. This absence is itself a finding, not a data gap.
  • VND→CNY direct conversion pricing is sparse and primarily available only at border province bank branches. Rates are not published nationally and vary by branch and counterparty.
  • Border trade informal pricing (traders operating outside formal banking channels) is undocumented in this report. Informal hawala-equivalent networks exist but are not quantified or recommended.
  • SBV daily central reference rate is used as the benchmark mid-market rate. Actual interbank rates may diverge within the +/–5% band.
  • WorldFirst VN tier pricing is based on published rate schedules. Actual fees for specific accounts may vary based on volume, account age, and product mix. The 0.40% China rate applies to clients sending $500K+/month equivalent.
  • All transfer cost estimates are for standard commercial transactions with complete documentation. Complex transactions, L/C-backed payments, or high-compliance-risk counterparties may carry additional charges.
  • Data collected April 2026. Exchange rates, regulatory requirements, and provider pricing are subject to change. Next scheduled review: October 2026.
