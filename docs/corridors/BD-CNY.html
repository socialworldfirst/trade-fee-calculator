Bangladesh is structurally different from every other corridor in this research series. There is no meaningful fintech comparison to make: Wise explicitly states “You can’t currently send money from BDT.” bKash, Bangladesh’s dominant mobile money platform with 80 million users, is receive-only for international transfers. Nagad operates identically. The World Bank does not collect outbound payment data for Bangladesh as a sending country.

What exists is a bank-dominated corridor structured almost entirely around Letters of Credit. Approximately 90% of Bangladesh–China trade settles in USD, not BDT. There is no BDT-CNY interbank market. Every transaction routes BDT→USD→CNY, incurring double conversion costs at each leg. Bangladesh Bank (Bangladesh’s central bank) maintains among the strictest currency controls in Asia.

WorldFirst’s estimated cost at P1 tier maximum published rates is approximately 3–4.5% — comparable to or slightly above the bank route on headline cost. WorldFirst’s value proposition in this corridor is not cost savings but process: speed, no LC requirement for eligible transactions, 1688 integration, and multi-currency holding.

§3 — Key Findings

1
Bangladesh is the ultimate fintech desert for outbound payments. Wise explicitly states “You can’t currently send money from BDT.” bKash (80 million users) and Nagad are receive-only for international transfers. The World Bank cannot collect outbound data from Bangladesh as a sending country. No global fintech has entered this market on the outbound side as of April 2026.
2
Letters of Credit are mandatory for most Bangladesh imports. Back-to-back LCs dominate the garment sector, where a Bangladeshi factory receives an LC from a Western buyer and uses it to open a second LC to pay the Chinese raw material supplier. This adds weeks of processing time and 0.1–0.5% LC commission on top of transfer costs — a structural overhead invisible in simple fee comparisons.
3
Approximately 90% of Bangladesh–China trade settles in USD. There is no BDT-CNY interbank market — all conversions route BDT→USD→CNY (double conversion). The buyer pays a markup at the BDT-to-USD conversion; the Chinese supplier’s bank applies a second spread at USD-to-CNY receipt.
4
Bangladesh authorized yuan for trade settlement in September 2022 and added CNY to its Real-Time Gross Settlement (RTGS) system in February 2024. Despite these policy moves, Bangladeshi banks have not opened yuan settlement accounts at scale. BDT-CNY direct settlement volume is near zero as of April 2026. The infrastructure exists on paper; it does not yet function in practice.
5
No bilateral currency swap agreement exists between Bangladesh and China. Discussions have been reported in financial media but no agreement has been signed as of April 2026. A swap agreement would be a prerequisite for meaningful BDT-CNY direct settlement at scale.
6
Bangladesh is the world’s second-largest garment exporter, with approximately $47 billion in annual exports. China supplies approximately 78% of Bangladesh’s fabric imports, totalling approximately $18.4 billion per year. The garment factory is the natural WorldFirst customer: it receives USD from Western buyers (H&M, Zara, Primark) and needs to pay Chinese raw material suppliers. This is not a retail corridor — it is an industrial supply chain payment corridor.
7
BRAC Bank’s Visa Direct partnership (2025) is the only notable payment innovation in this corridor. It enables faster outward remittance by bypassing traditional correspondent banking chains. Dutch-Bangla Bank’s NexusPay platform offers domestic digital payments but does not extend to outbound international transfers. No other bank-side innovation has been identified for this corridor.
8
BDT is not freely convertible. Bangladesh Bank’s controls are among the strictest in Asia: SMEs face a $3,000/year outward remittance limit without prior approval, all import payments require Bangladesh Bank authorization, and a 4-month payment deadline applies from the date of import. These controls mean that most Bangladeshi importers engage the formal banking system for every transaction — there is no informal or fintech bypass available.

§4 — Cost Breakdown by Route

There is only one route for this corridor: USD. BDT-CNY direct settlement is a policy aspiration, not a functional payment pathway. No fintech comparison is possible. The table below reflects bank-side costs only.

The BDT→USD→CNY Double Conversion Chain

All Bangladesh-to-China payments follow the same chain regardless of which bank is used. The buyer pays BDT to their Bangladeshi bank, which converts to USD and sends via SWIFT. The Chinese supplier’s bank receives USD and converts to CNY. Three cost layers apply — buyers are typically aware of only the first two.

Layer 3 — FX Risk Buffer Embedded in USD Prices (Buyer Pays, Indirectly)

Chinese suppliers typically embed a 2–5% FX risk buffer into USD-quoted prices to hedge against exchange rate movements between quotation and settlement. This practice applies equally to Bangladeshi buyers as it does to Western buyers. The buffer is invisible on the invoice but present in the unit price. For Bangladesh’s garment factories — operating on thin margins — this is a material cost.

The correct comparison for WorldFirst in Bangladesh is not “WF vs. bank wire fees” but “WF vs. the LC process.” A garment factory opening a back-to-back LC pays: LC commission (0.1–0.5%), SWIFT fees ($12–33), FX markup (0.5–1.5%), plus 2–6 weeks of working capital tied up in the LC process. §7 — Switching Analysis

Bangladesh is the only corridor in this research series where the switching analysis does not show a clear cost saving for moving to WorldFirst. Bank average cost is approximately 3%; WorldFirst at P1 maximum is approximately 3–4.5%. On headline percentage, WorldFirst is comparable to or slightly more expensive than the bank route.

This makes Bangladesh unique: the case for WorldFirst is not “save X% on every transfer.” The case is structural and operational.

The Real Comparison
WorldFirst vs. the LC Process, Not WorldFirst vs. Bank Wire Fees
A Bangladeshi garment factory doing back-to-back LCs to pay Chinese fabric suppliers is not just paying 2–4% in fees. It is tying up working capital for 4–8 weeks per transaction, paying LC commission on top of wire fees, managing documentation requirements (bill of lading, commercial invoice, certificate of origin), and absorbing the risk that the LC opening bank rejects documents on a technicality. WorldFirst eliminates all of this for transactions where LC is not contractually required by the Chinese supplier. The speed difference alone (1–2 days vs. 4–8 weeks) is worth more than the fee difference to a factory managing tight production schedules.
Scenario A
Garment Factory — Large LC Transactions ($50k+)
Continue bank LC for contractually-required LC transactions. Use WorldFirst for supplementary direct payments (samples, rush orders, accessory suppliers) where LC is not required. WorldFirst as a secondary payment channel alongside the primary bank relationship.
Scenario B
SME Importer — Under $50k Transactions
WorldFirst may be the only viable non-LC option for small transactions. Bangladesh Bank’s $3,000/year SME outward remittance cap applies to informal channels; formal bank LC can process larger amounts. WorldFirst at 3–4.5% vs. bank at 2–4% is a modest premium for a significantly faster, less documentation-intensive process. If the customer qualifies and has a WorldFirst account, this is likely the better operational choice for sub-$50k transactions.
Scenario C
1688 Buyers
WorldFirst World Pay is the functional requirement, not a preference. 1688 (Alibaba’s domestic B2B platform) does not accept bank wires or LCs from foreign buyers. WorldFirst’s 1688 integration is the only way for a Bangladeshi importer to purchase directly from 1688 suppliers. In this scenario, WorldFirst is not competing on cost — it is the only option.
Scenario D
Personal / Individual Remittance
No viable outbound options exist for individuals sending money from Bangladesh to China for personal purposes. Banks require business documentation. No fintech serves this use case. This corridor is effectively closed for personal outbound transfers.

§9 — Frequently Asked Questions

Are Letters of Credit required for all Bangladesh imports from China?
Not legally required for all imports, but effectively mandatory for most formal trade. Bangladesh Bank regulations require prior authorization for outward remittances. For large transactions with established suppliers, most Bangladeshi banks will only process the payment via LC or LC-equivalent documentation. Smaller transactions ($3,000 and below) fall within the SME outward remittance limit and may be processed as direct bank wires, but this is the exception rather than the rule. Non-LC options like WorldFirst are growing but remain a small fraction of the market.
Why does no fintech serve Bangladesh for outbound payments?
Multiple structural reasons: (1) BDT is not freely convertible — Bangladesh Bank controls mean that any fintech processing outward BDT remittances must be licensed by Bangladesh Bank, creating high regulatory compliance costs. (2) The SME market size is limited compared to the compliance investment required. (3) The informal remittance market (hundi) historically handled smaller outflows, reducing demand for licensed fintech channels. (4) The dominant fintech players (Wise, Airwallex, Revolut) have prioritized freely-convertible currencies where volume is larger and regulatory friction lower. Bangladesh is on the list, not at the top.
Bangladesh authorized yuan settlement and added CNY to RTGS. Why does BDT-CNY direct settlement not work?
Policy authorization and operational implementation are different things. Bangladesh Bank authorized yuan for trade settlement in September 2022 and added CNY to the RTGS system in February 2024. However, Bangladeshi banks have not opened yuan settlement accounts with Chinese correspondent banks at scale, because: (1) there is no bilateral currency swap agreement to provide BDT/CNY liquidity; (2) the BDT has limited international demand, making yuan liquidity difficult to source; and (3) banks require a minimum volume threshold to justify the operational investment. As of April 2026, BDT-CNY direct settlement is near zero in practice.
What is BRAC Bank’s Visa Direct partnership and does it reduce costs?
BRAC Bank announced a Visa Direct partnership in 2025 enabling faster outward remittance by routing through Visa’s payment network rather than traditional correspondent banking chains. This primarily reduces processing time and may reduce correspondent bank intermediary fees ($20–50 typically). It does not materially reduce the FX markup component, which constitutes the majority of transfer cost. The Visa Direct partnership is a speed and reliability improvement, not primarily a cost reduction. Its scope and the specific transactions it covers have not been independently verified.
How do Bangladesh’s garment factories pay their Chinese fabric suppliers?
The dominant mechanism is the back-to-back Letter of Credit. A Western buyer (H&M, Primark, Zara) opens an LC in favour of the Bangladeshi garment factory. The factory uses this LC as collateral to open a second (back-to-back) LC in favour of its Chinese fabric supplier. The Chinese supplier ships fabric, presents documents to their bank, and receives payment. The entire chain takes 4–8 weeks and involves four banks: the Western buyer’s bank, the Bangladeshi factory’s bank, the Bangladesh Bank clearing channel, and the Chinese supplier’s bank. WorldFirst and similar fintechs represent a potential alternative for the second leg (Bangladesh factory to Chinese supplier), but only for transactions where the Chinese supplier accepts non-LC payment.
Is BDT freely convertible? What are Bangladesh Bank’s key controls?
BDT is not freely convertible. Key Bangladesh Bank controls relevant to outbound payments include: (1) all outward remittances require authorization — banks must approve transactions before funds leave the country; (2) SME outward remittance cap of $3,000 per year without prior Bangladesh Bank approval; (3) all import payments require documentation proving a legitimate import transaction (invoice, bill of lading, letter of credit, or equivalent); (4) a 4-month payment deadline — imports must be paid within 4 months of shipment; and (5) proceeds from export receipts must be repatriated within a specified timeframe. These controls make Bangladesh one of the most regulated outbound payment environments in Asia.
How does WorldFirst’s cost compare to Bangladeshi banks?
On direct fee comparison, WorldFirst at maximum P1 rates (3–4.5%) is comparable to or slightly above Bangladeshi bank rates (2–4%). This is the only corridor in this research series where WorldFirst does not show a clear cost advantage on headline percentage. However, the comparison is incomplete if it excludes LC overhead. When LC commission (0.1–0.5%), documentation costs, and the working capital cost of funds tied up in the LC process are included, WorldFirst’s total economic cost for eligible transactions is likely lower than the full-cycle bank cost. WorldFirst’s 1–2 day settlement vs. bank LC’s 4–8 weeks also represents a material working capital benefit, particularly for factories operating on thin margins and tight production schedules.
What is the $3,000 SME outward remittance facility?
Bangladesh Bank allows SMEs to remit up to $3,000 per year outward for trade-related purposes without requiring full LC documentation, subject to simplified remittance procedures. This facility is designed for small importers who cannot afford the overhead of opening a full LC for small purchases. In practice, $3,000/year is a very tight limit for any regular importer. For transactions above this threshold, full Bangladesh Bank authorization and LC documentation are typically required. WorldFirst, for customers who can establish an account and qualify under Bangladesh Bank regulations, may be a viable channel for these smaller-value, simplified-remittance transactions.

Approximately 90% of Bangladesh–China trade settles in USD. There is no BDT-CNY interbank market. USD has been the standard for this corridor since the 1980s and despite Bangladesh’s 2022 yuan authorization and 2024 RTGS addition, the practical settlement share of CNY is near zero. USD dominance is structural and self-reinforcing: Chinese suppliers quote in USD, Bangladeshi banks price in USD, and LC documentation defaults to USD.
