Corridor-specific note: ING's published koersopslag (rate surcharge) of 0.85% is the most competitive traditional bank rate found across all 10 corridors researched. At EUR 91 on EUR 10,000, ING sits within range of some fintechs. WorldFirst at EUR 115 is EUR 24 more expensive than ING for a standard transfer. This is an honest finding. The WorldFirst case rests on 1688.com payments, Alipay/WeChat delivery, and volume users — not on raw FX price.

Dutch bank transfers to China vary dramatically by institution. ING's 0.85% koersopslag — sourced from Refinitiv/WM Reuters and applied uniformly — makes it the only traditional bank in this research approaching fintech pricing. ABN AMRO's markup of 1.5–5% is more typical of the European banking benchmark. The Netherlands has no designated CNY clearing center; all EUR→CNY conversions route through Frankfurt's Bank of China clearing hub.

Fintech providers divide into two tiers. Wise (via balance funding) and Airwallex reach the corridor's lower bound at approximately EUR 53–60 on EUR 10,000. A second tier — OFX, Revolut (higher plans), and WorldFirst — sits in the EUR 100–150 range. WorldFirst's China-specific features, including 1688 World Pay integration and Alipay/WeChat direct delivery, differentiate it from the cost-only comparison.

Two Dutch neobanks — Bunq and N26 (German-licensed but strongly present in the Netherlands) — both power their international transfers through Wise, effectively providing mid-market rate transfers through a banking interface. Revolut holds a Lithuanian e-money license and serves a substantial Dutch user base.

§3 — Key Findings

1
ING's published koersopslag of 0.85% makes it the most competitive traditional bank found across all 10 corridors researched. On EUR 10,000, ING costs approximately EUR 91 (0.85% markup plus EUR 6 wire fee) — within range of some fintech providers and significantly below the European bank average of approximately 2.5–4%.
2
The Netherlands runs the EU's largest trade deficit with China, estimated at approximately EUR 72.4 billion in 2024. This figure is substantially inflated by the Rotterdam Effect: goods entering Europe through Port of Rotterdam are counted as Dutch imports in trade statistics even when their final destination is Germany, France, or elsewhere. The true bilateral Dutch–Chinese trade volume is considerably lower.
3
ABN AMRO restricts CNY payments to business-to-business transactions only, citing PBOC and SAFE regulatory requirements. The bank maintains a dedicated China transfer page requiring CNAPS codes (Chinese National Advanced Payment System routing identifiers) for all CNY-denominated payments. Personal CNY remittances are not supported.
4
Bunq, the Amsterdam-headquartered neobank with 12 million+ users, integrates Wise for international transfers. Dutch users accessing CNY payments through Bunq are effectively transacting at Wise's mid-market rate model through a licensed banking interface — without opening a Wise account directly.
5
The Netherlands has no designated RMB clearing center. Frankfurt — home of the Eurozone's primary Bank of China RMB clearing hub — services Dutch EUR–CNY transactions. This adds one routing step compared to corridors with domestic clearing (e.g., the UK corridor, where London serves as a global RMB hub).
6
ASML (headquartered in Veldhoven) created a unique, high-value NL–China corridor dimension. In Q2 2024, 49% of ASML's total revenue came from Chinese customers before export controls expanded. Single transactions in this corridor can reach hundreds of millions of euros, representing a structurally different transfer profile from SME importing.
7
Chinese state-owned companies hold approximately 8.2% of Rotterdam port container capacity, including stakes by COSCO, China Merchants, and Hutchison. The port's operational dependency on Chinese capital creates a structural dimension to the NL–China financial relationship that extends beyond trade invoicing.
8
The Dutch regulatory environment is among the lightest globally for outbound payments. There are zero capital controls on EUR transfers. Reporting requirements under the Wet financiële betrekkingen buitenland (AWV) are statistical-only: the threshold was raised to EUR 50,000 per transaction in January 2025, meaning the vast majority of SME transfers require no AWV filing. DNB (De Nederlandsche Bank) and AFM operate a twin-peaks model.

§4 — Cost Breakdown by Route

Approximately 50–55% of Netherlands–China SME trade settles in USD, as the US dollar remains the dominant invoicing currency for manufactured goods from China. Dutch buyers convert EUR→USD at their bank, wire USD via SWIFT, and the Chinese supplier's bank converts USD→CNY on receipt. Three cost layers apply.

Layer 3 — FX Risk Buffer in USD-Quoted Prices (Buyer Pays, Indirectly)

Chinese suppliers embed an estimated 2–5% currency risk buffer into USD-quoted prices to protect against EUR/USD volatility between order confirmation and payment settlement. This cost is structural and invisible on the invoice but present in unit pricing. Switching to CNY direct payment eliminates the rationale for this buffer and has been reported to reduce effective unit prices by 1–3%.

§8 — Decision Framework

Scenario A
ING customers — standard EUR→CNY bank transfers
Stay with ING. At 0.85% koersopslag and EUR 6 fee, ING is cheaper than WorldFirst (EUR 91 vs EUR 115 on EUR 10,000). There is no price-based case for switching to WorldFirst on standard transfers from ING. Consider WorldFirst only if you need 1688.com direct payment, Alipay/WeChat delivery, or the World Card's zero-FX spending on multiple currencies.
Scenario B
ABN AMRO or Rabobank customers — any transfer volume
Switch to WorldFirst or Wise. At ABN AMRO's mid-rate of ~3.25%, the saving on EUR 10,000 is EUR 219 per transfer (EUR 2,628/year at monthly frequency). Even ABN AMRO's best-case rate of 1.5% costs EUR 159 vs WorldFirst EUR 115. Rabobank's combined rate of ~3.1% produces EUR 195 per transfer saving. The case is strong at any regular transfer frequency.
Scenario C
1688.com buyers — factory-price direct purchasing from China
WorldFirst World Pay is the only non-bank solution with native 1688.com integration. At 0.80% total, it enables direct factory-price purchasing without an agent or intermediary. No other provider in this research set offers equivalent 1688 access. This use case has no meaningful competitor outside the WorldFirst ecosystem.
Scenario D
B2B regular importers — high volume, speed-sensitive
Airwallex at ~0.6% (EUR 60 on EUR 10,000) offers the best price with same-day local CNY delivery, subject to recipient eligibility (Chinese-national legal representative or UBO required). WorldFirst at 1.15% combined is the alternative for importers whose suppliers don't meet Airwallex's eligibility criteria, or who value Alipay/WeChat delivery and 1688 access alongside standard bank transfers.
Scenario E
Personal or small-value transfers — occasional senders

§9 — Frequently Asked Questions

Is ING really cheaper than Wise for sending money to China?
For most standard transfers, ING at EUR 91 is slightly more expensive than Wise via balance funding (EUR 53) but significantly cheaper than Wise via bank debit (EUR 148) or credit card. The comparison depends on how Wise is funded. ING at 0.85% koersopslag is the most competitive traditional bank rate found in all 10 corridors researched — but it is still a SWIFT-only route with 2–5 day delivery, no same-day CNY, and no Alipay/WeChat delivery.
What is the Rotterdam Effect and why does it make Dutch trade statistics misleading?
The Rotterdam Effect refers to the statistical distortion caused by Port of Rotterdam's role as Europe's largest port and primary entry point for containerized goods from Asia. Under EU trade statistics methodology, goods imported through Rotterdam are recorded as Dutch imports regardless of their ultimate EU destination. A container of Chinese electronics arriving at Rotterdam and then trucked to a German distribution center is counted as a Dutch import, not a German one. The Netherlands' reported EUR 72.4B trade deficit with China in 2024 is substantially inflated by this effect. True bilateral Dutch–Chinese trade — goods consumed in the Netherlands — is considerably lower.
Why does ABN AMRO restrict CNY payments to business-to-business only?
ABN AMRO cites PBOC (People's Bank of China) and SAFE (State Administration of Foreign Exchange) regulatory requirements as the basis for restricting CNY payments to B2B transactions. China's cross-border capital account remains partially controlled, and SAFE sets limits and reporting requirements on inbound foreign currency conversions. Retail personal remittances in CNY carry higher compliance risk and lower volume, and ABN AMRO has chosen to exclude them from its service rather than build the compliance infrastructure. Personal CNY transfers are available through Wise, Revolut, and Bunq.
Can I send money to China through bunq and get a good rate?
Yes. Bunq's international transfers are powered by Wise, which applies a 0% FX markup plus a transparent fee (approximately 0.53% from Wise balance equivalent). Bunq users accessing CNY transfers through the Bunq app receive rates comparable to using Wise directly, without opening a separate Wise account. A Bunq subscription (EUR 10.99–45/month) is required. For frequent senders, the subscription cost should be factored into the true per-transfer cost.
Do I need to file an AWV report when sending money to China from the Netherlands?
As of January 2025, AWV (Wet financiële betrekkingen buitenland) reporting to De Nederlandsche Bank is required only for individual transactions above EUR 50,000. Reporting is statistical in nature — it informs balance-of-payments data — and carries no approval requirement or capital control mechanism. For SME importers making monthly transfers under EUR 50,000, no AWV filing is required. Dutch banks handle AWV reporting automatically for transactions above the threshold. No prior approval is required for any outbound EUR transfer to China under Dutch law.
What is the cheapest way to send EUR 10,000 to China from the Netherlands?
Wise via Wise Balance funding at approximately EUR 53 (0.53% effective) is the lowest cost at EUR 10,000. Airwallex at approximately EUR 60 (0.6%) is second and offers same-day local CNY delivery for eligible B2B recipients. ING at EUR 91 is the cheapest traditional bank option. WorldFirst at EUR 115 is more expensive than these three but provides 1688.com access, Alipay/WeChat delivery, and multi-currency World Account functionality not available from the cheaper options. Airwallex's B2B eligibility restriction (Chinese-national legal representative required) means some importers cannot access the EUR 60 rate.
How does the ASML–China corridor affect the NL-CNY payment market?
ASML (Veldhoven, Netherlands) is the world's sole manufacturer of extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines, which are essential for producing advanced semiconductors. In Q2 2024, 49% of ASML's total revenue came from Chinese customers. Single ASML transactions to China can reach hundreds of millions of euros. This high-value flow is entirely separate from SME importing and is typically handled through direct banking relationships with ABN AMRO, ING, and international clearing banks rather than through fintech providers. Export control expansion (2023 Dutch government restrictions on deep UV machines to China) has begun to reduce this flow, but the residual volumes remain structurally important to the NL–China banking corridor.
Can I pay my 1688.com suppliers from the Netherlands?
Yes, through WorldFirst's World Pay feature. WorldFirst is the only non-bank payment provider with native 1688.com integration accessible to Dutch users. The effective rate is approximately 0.80% total (combined FX and platform fee), and payment is made directly to the 1688.com supplier's account without the need for an agent, Alipay-linked Chinese account, or manual bank wire. No other provider in this research set — including ING, Wise, Airwallex, or Revolut — offers equivalent direct 1688.com payment capability.

The Netherlands–China corridor sits within the EU average for USD invoicing dominance in manufactured goods trade. EUR direct settlement is higher than the global average, reflecting the Eurozone's depth and the willingness of larger Chinese exporters serving European customers to invoice in EUR. CNY direct settlement at 5–8% is below Germany's (~10%) but above many non-EU corridors, consistent with the Netherlands' role as a major EU trade hub.

RMB Clearing Infrastructure

The Netherlands has no designated RMB clearing center. Frankfurt (Germany) hosts the Eurozone's primary Bank of China RMB offshore clearing hub, established in 2014. Dutch EUR–CNY conversions and CNY payments route through Frankfurt before reaching the Chinese banking system. This adds one routing step compared to corridors with domestic clearing (London for GBP, Paris for France post-2020). The absence of a Dutch clearing center reflects the Netherlands' position as a trade hub rather than a financial center: Rotterdam processes goods, Frankfurt processes euros.

Trade Relationship

The Netherlands reports the EU's largest trade deficit with China at approximately EUR 72.4 billion in 2024, driven substantially by the Rotterdam Effect (see FAQ). Actual bilateral Dutch–Chinese trade is significantly lower. Key Dutch import categories from China: electronics and machinery (Rotterdam entrepot), industrial components, textiles and consumer goods. Key Dutch exports to China: agri-food (dairy, flowers, seed stock via Rabobank-financed supply chains), high-tech machinery (ASML), chemicals. Total adjusted bilateral trade estimated at EUR 110 billion when including transit trade.

ASML Factor

ASML's Veldhoven headquarters generates a structurally unusual high-value payment corridor. Before 2023 export control restrictions, Chinese semiconductor fabs (SMIC, YMTC, Hua Hong) were ASML's largest customer segment. Q2 2024 saw 49% revenue from China. Individual machine sales (deep UV DUV: approximately EUR 40–80M; extreme UV EUV: approximately EUR 150–200M per unit) create single-transaction CNY or USD payment flows that dwarf typical SME importing volumes. These flows transit through institutional banking relationships and are outside the scope of this SME-focused research, but they represent the structural foundation of the NL–China financial corridor.

Port of Rotterdam China Exposure

Chinese state-owned and state-linked companies hold approximately 8.2% of Rotterdam port container terminal capacity. COSCO Shipping (China Ocean Shipping Company), China Merchants Port Holdings, and CK Hutchison (Hong Kong, China-linked) all hold operational stakes. This creates a structural dependency between the Dutch port economy and Chinese logistics capital, and generates recurring EUR-denominated payments from Rotterdam terminal operators to Chinese parent entities — a distinct high-volume payment flow separate from importer-to-supplier transactions.

Regulatory Environment

The Netherlands operates a twin-peaks financial regulatory model: De Nederlandsche Bank (DNB) for prudential oversight, Autoriteit Financiële Markten (AFM) for conduct regulation. For outbound EUR payments to China: zero capital controls, AWV statistical reporting required only above EUR 50,000 (threshold raised January 2025), no prior approval required at any amount. DNB authorizes payment institutions and e-money institutions; the Netherlands has among the highest densities of licensed payment institutions in the EU, reflecting Amsterdam's position as a fintech hub. Bunq holds a full Dutch banking license; most fintechs operate under DNB e-money authorization or EU passport from BaFin/FCA/Lithuanian equivalent.
