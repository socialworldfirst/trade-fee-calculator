Section 03
KEY FINDINGS
01
Most underserved major corridor globally. Nigeria-China handles an estimated $24.9B in annual trade yet both Monito and the World Bank report zero qualifying comparison provider data for NGN-CNY. The corridor exists largely outside formal tracking infrastructure.
02
Africa-China fintech wave, 2025–2026. A cluster of purpose-built operators launched on this corridor in the 2025–2026 window: Pesa (zero fees, Alipay delivery, minutes), Klasha (5–10 minute settlement), Importa Pay (8-hour B2B, $25–45 flat fee), Clea (stablecoin-enabled, same-day). These are the cheapest formal-channel options available.
03
Nigeria's multi-rate FX system redefines "cost." The effective cost of a transfer depends on which FX rate the sender accesses. The CBN-parallel spread ranges from 1.5% during calm periods to over 30% during stress episodes — most recently when the black market hit N1,900/$ in March 2026 against an official rate near N1,382/$. The same transfer can cost 2x more depending on market conditions and the sender's FX access tier.
04
WorldFirst is not price-competitive on this corridor. P1 tier pricing (1.5% FX + 3% payment = 4.5–6%) is higher than both Nigerian banks and all Africa-China fintechs. WF's advantages — compliance depth, multi-currency accounts, 1688 Pay integration — are real, but they do not translate to cost leadership here. Honest positioning matters.
05
Form M is mandatory and adds weeks. The CBN's Form M requirement applies to ALL imports without exception. Before any payment can be initiated, the importer must file Form M through a licensed bank, obtain CBN approval, and register the transaction. Processing adds 2–6 weeks of lead time. This is not a fintech workaround — it applies regardless of payment channel.
06
43 restricted product categories cannot access CBN FX windows. The CBN's restricted items list covers 43 categories including textiles, furniture, footwear, toothpicks, and selected electronics. Importers of these goods must self-source FX at parallel rates, materially increasing their effective transfer cost. The restriction does not prevent importation — it prevents accessing official FX support for it.
07
Informal channels and stablecoins handle significant volume. USDT/USDC-based corridors compete aggressively on speed and cost. Informal agents (known locally as "bureau de change plus" operators) also route significant cross-border volume. Neither channel provides regulatory protection for senders. In March 2026, CBN signaled it would tighten enforcement on crypto-facilitated FX flows.
08
Nigeria-China currency swap enables limited cheap access. The $2B NGN/CNY bilateral currency swap (renewed December 2024) enables CBN to conduct yuan-settlement auctions at approximately 2% spread. Access is limited to CBN-designated commercial banks only, and auction supply is irregular. Volume is insufficient to serve the full corridor.
09
Trade imbalance context. China exported a record $24.9B to Nigeria in 2025. Nigeria ran its largest trade deficit with any partner country — N17.01 trillion — exclusively with China. This is one-directional: payment flows are almost entirely NG→CN. The structural imbalance creates sustained, high-volume demand for this corridor with no natural balancing mechanism.

Section 09
FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS — NIGERIA-SPECIFIC
What is CBN Form M and why does it matter for China payments?
Form M is the Central Bank of Nigeria's mandatory import declaration form. It must be filed through a licensed commercial bank before any import payment can be processed. Processing takes 2–6 weeks and requires product documentation, supplier invoices, and bank approval. It applies to all import payments regardless of size, channel, or product category. No payment provider — bank or fintech — can bypass Form M. It is the single largest lead-time factor on this corridor.
Why is Wise not available on the Nigeria-to-China corridor?
Wise does not currently support NGN as a send currency for business payment transfers. Nigeria's complex multi-rate FX system, CBN licensing requirements, and Form M pre-clearance requirements create regulatory compliance barriers that Wise has not navigated for this corridor. Wise is fully available for receiving USD/GBP/EUR in Nigeria, but not for sending NGN to China. This gap is precisely what Africa-China fintechs like Pesa and Klasha launched to fill.
What is Nigeria's multi-rate FX system and how does it affect transfer costs?
Nigeria maintains multiple effective exchange rates simultaneously. The CBN official rate (currently ~N1,382/$) applies to approved transactions. The parallel (black market) rate trades higher — it reached N1,900/$ in March 2026 during a stress episode. Importers accessing official FX through CBN-approved channels pay at the official rate. Importers whose goods fall on the restricted list (43 categories) or who cannot access CBN FX windows must source USD at parallel rates. This can add 30%+ to effective transfer costs, completely dwarfing any fintech fee comparison.
What is the cheapest way to send money from Nigeria to China right now?
Based on April 2026 data, Pesa (zero transfer fee, ~1–2% FX spread, Alipay delivery in minutes) and Importa Pay ($25–45 flat + ~1.5% FX, 8-hour B2B settlement) offer the lowest total cost on formal rails. Clea's stablecoin bridge may be cheaper still (~1–2%) but carries regulatory risk. The answer changes materially depending on which FX rate the sender can access — a 30% parallel premium negates all fintech fee savings.
Are Klasha and Pesa reliable enough to use for business payments?
Both launched in 2025 and have limited track records relative to established banks or WorldFirst. Klasha has received venture backing and published operational metrics (5–10 minute settlement). Pesa's zero-fee model is confirmed from user reports and rate comparisons. The honest answer is: they are promising but not yet proven at enterprise scale. SME importers have used both successfully. For high-value transactions ($100k+) or where delivery failure is business-critical, a more established provider is lower risk even if more expensive.
What are CBN's 43 restricted items and how do they affect my payment?
The CBN restricted items list (CBN circular) covers 43 product categories including: textile fabrics, furniture, footwear, toothpicks, mosquito repellent coils, soap, ballpoint pens, selected electronics, and others. Importers of restricted items cannot access CBN FX windows — they must source USD at parallel market rates. The restriction does not legally prohibit importing the goods; it restricts access to official FX support. Importers should check the current CBN list before assuming official FX access is available for their product category.
What is the difference between formal and informal channels on this corridor?
Formal channels are CBN-licensed banks and registered fintechs. They involve Form M, official FX, and regulatory oversight. Informal channels include USDT/USDC stablecoin corridors and cash bureau de change agents who route payments without CBN oversight. Informal channels are faster and often cheaper during stress periods. They offer no regulatory protection: if a payment fails, there is no CBN recourse, no SWIFT trace, and no legal remedy. Significant import volume moves through informal channels — particularly for restricted goods — but the risk profile is fundamentally different.
Is using USDT/stablecoins to pay Chinese suppliers legal in Nigeria?
The legal status is ambiguous and deteriorating. CBN broadly prohibits Nigerian banks from facilitating crypto transactions (2021 circular). In March 2026, CBN signaled expanded enforcement targeting stablecoin-facilitated FX flows. Informal use is widespread and enforcement is inconsistent. For Nigerian businesses, stablecoin payment to Chinese suppliers carries legal risk — it is not a recommended practice for formal or regulated entities. The enforcement environment is changing and the direction is toward tighter restriction.

CORRIDOR CONTEXT — NIGERIA FX SYSTEM & REGULATIONS
CBN FX Controls — Active Restrictions
  • Form M: Mandatory pre-payment import declaration. All imports, all values, all channels. 2–6 week processing minimum.
  • 43 Restricted Items: Cannot access CBN FX windows. Self-sourcing at parallel rates required. List includes textiles, furniture, footwear, electronics (selected).
  • Cash Limit: $10,000 cash limit for travel FX per CBN regulations.
  • Card Limit: Naira debit/credit cards capped at $20/day international usage (as of 2023 policy, subject to revision).
  • May 2026 Naira-Only Remittance Rule: CBN signaled requirement for diaspora remittances to be received in NGN only. Direction of travel is tighter FX controls.
  • Crypto/Stablecoin: Banks prohibited from facilitating crypto transactions (2021). Expanded enforcement signaled March 2026.
  • CBN Form M Processing Banks: Only licensed commercial banks can file Form M. Fintechs must partner with a licensed bank for this step.

DATA LIMITATIONS & CONFIDENCE ASSESSMENT
Research Confidence: LOW-MEDIUM
The Nigeria-China corridor has the thinnest available formal data of all corridors in this research series. The limitations below are material and should be read before acting on any specific cost figure.
!
Zero comparison platform coverage. Neither Monito nor the World Bank Remittance Prices database track qualifying providers for NGN-CNY. All cost estimates are reconstructed from provider rate pages, importer reports, and secondary market research — not live comparison data.
!
Nigerian bank fees are not publicly published. Zenith, First Bank, GTBank, Access, and UBA do not disclose FX spreads or international wire fees on public rate sheets. Estimates are based on importer community reports, Nigerian consumer finance forums, and financial press. Actual fees may differ by customer relationship tier.
!
Africa-China fintech pricing is largely undisclosed. Pesa, Klasha, Importa Pay, and Clea all launched in 2025–2026. Their pricing pages are either not public, or present indicative rates rather than guaranteed charges. Rate spreads are approximated from published indicatives and user reports. Costs may change rapidly as these companies scale.
!
Informal channel costs are unmeasurable. Stablecoin corridors and informal agent networks handle material volume but leave no formal data trace. Their effective cost range is not included in provider comparisons, as it would require active research into illegal or grey-market channels.
!
Multi-rate FX system makes "cost" definition context-dependent. A transfer at the official CBN rate and a transfer at the parallel rate look entirely different in cost percentage terms. Published "cost" figures assume official FX access. For restricted goods importers, actual costs are materially higher. No single cost figure accurately represents the full corridor.
!
CBN regulations are changing rapidly. The May 2026 naira-only remittance policy, March 2026 stablecoin enforcement signal, and Form M procedural changes are recent. The regulatory landscape may shift materially between the date of this research (April 2026) and the date of use. All compliance information should be verified against current CBN circulars.
!
Provider viability for new Africa-China fintechs is unverified. Pesa, Klasha, Importa Pay, and Clea are early-stage. While their cost positioning is compelling, their operational reliability at scale, regulatory standing in both Nigeria and China, and long-term viability are not independently verified. Cost estimates are research-grade, not financial advice.

WorldFirst is not the cheapest option on this corridor — it is more expensive than both Nigerian commercial banks and purpose-built Africa-China fintechs on a pure cost basis. WorldFirst's competitive advantages are: direct CNY settlement via CNAPS (bypassing SWIFT), multi-currency account infrastructure (NGN, USD, CNY), 1688 marketplace Pay integration (unique on the corridor), and compliance depth.

Nigeria's multi-rate FX system creates additional cost variability. The CBN official rate stands at approximately N1,382/USD as of April 2026. The parallel market rate reached N1,900/USD in March 2026 — a 37% premium representing a 37+ percentage point addition to any transfer cost for senders unable to access official FX windows. 43 CBN-restricted product categories (including textiles, furniture, footwear, and selected electronics) cannot access official FX, forcing self-sourcing at parallel rates. CBN Form M is mandatory for all imports, adding 2–6 weeks of processing before any payment can be initiated.

Nigeria runs its largest bilateral trade deficit exclusively with China: N17.01 trillion in 2025. China exported a record $24.9B to Nigeria in 2025. A $2B NGN/CNY bilateral currency swap (renewed December 2024) enables CBN yuan auctions at approximately 2% spread, with access limited to designated commercial banks.
