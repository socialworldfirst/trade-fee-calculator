SA-CNY — Saudi Arabia to China

3 April 2026  ·  Mid-market: 1 SAR = 1.942 CNY  ·  1 USD = 3.75 SAR (pegged)  ·  9 Providers
Corridor Research Version 1.0 Next Review: October 2026
The petrodollar corridor. Saudi Arabia is China’s largest oil supplier and China is Saudi Arabia’s #1 trade partner ($107.5B bilateral trade, 2024). The riyal’s hard peg to USD at 3.75 means SAR→CNY is effectively a USD→CNY conversion with an extra layer of bank fees. Most fintechs cannot send from Saudi Arabia — Wise does not support outbound SAR. The corridor is dominated by traditional banks and exchange houses, with petro-yuan developments and Vision 2030 fintech reforms creating slow but structural change.
Headline
SAR is pegged to USD. The FX cost is hidden in the SAR→USD leg that barely moves, plus the real markup on USD→CNY.

Because 1 USD = 3.75 SAR is fixed, Saudi banks treat USD as a near-domestic currency with tight spreads (~0.1–0.3%). The real cost appears on the USD→CNY conversion, where banks apply 1.4–2.3% markup. Total bank cost for a SAR 37,500 (~$10,000) transfer to China runs ~2.5–4.0% including fees. Not the most expensive corridor, but the fintech desert for outbound transfers means limited alternatives. Exchange houses (Al Rajhi Exchange/Tahweel, Enjaz) serve remittance workers but cap at personal amounts. B2B payments route almost exclusively through SWIFT.

Settlement Currency Composition

China is Saudi Arabia’s #1 trade partner. Saudi imports from China reached $55.7B in 2024 (electronics, machinery, vehicles, textiles). Saudi exports to China: $57B, over 80% crude oil. Settlement is overwhelmingly USD, with petro-yuan developments still in early stages despite the November 2023 PBOC-SAMA currency swap agreement (RMB 50B / SAR 26B).

Layer 1 — Saudi Bank SAR→USD Spread

Because of the USD peg, SAR→USD spreads are among the tightest in the world: ~0.1–0.3%. This is effectively a rounding cost, not a true FX conversion. Banks make their money on the transfer fee and the markup they embed in the onward USD→CNY pricing (for banks that offer CNY) or the correspondent chain.

Layer 2 — Chinese Bank USD→CNY (Supplier Side)

Standard across all USD-routed corridors: the supplier’s Chinese bank converts incoming USD to CNY at 0.3–1.0% markup. $10–30 in intermediary deductions may also apply depending on the correspondent chain (1–2 intermediary banks typical for Saudi→China SWIFT).

Layer 3 — FX Risk Buffer (Supplier Pricing)

Chinese suppliers typically add 2–5% buffer to USD-quoted prices to cover FX movement risk between quoting and payment. Because SAR is pegged to USD, there is no additional volatility premium from the Saudi side — unlike Turkey or Nigeria. This is a structural advantage of the SAR corridor: suppliers do not add a “Saudi premium” on top of the standard USD buffer.

Decision Framework

Scenario A
SME importing from China — Regular supplier payments ($5k–$50k)
WorldFirst offers the lowest total cost (~2.25%) with same-day CNY delivery and zero intermediary deductions. The 1688 World Pay option (1.45%) is even cheaper for Alibaba-sourced goods. Al Rajhi Bank is the next best at ~2.5–3.5% but takes 2–5 days. The elimination of supplier FX risk buffer (2–5%) when paying in CNY creates additional savings not reflected in the direct cost comparison.
Scenario B
Personal remittance — Worker sending money home to China
Tahweel Al Rajhi (SAR 17 via app) or Western Union (SAR 15+ online) for speed and convenience. WorldRemit is another option for smaller amounts. Note: Chinese expatriate population in Saudi Arabia is small relative to South Asian communities. Most remittance infrastructure is optimized for India, Pakistan, Philippines, and Egypt, not China.
Scenario C
Large corporate / oil settlement
Institutional channels through SNB, Al Rajhi, or direct via Bank of China / ICBC Riyadh branches. Negotiated FX rates available for large volumes. The PBOC-SAMA swap line (RMB 50B) theoretically enables direct SAR→CNY settlement at institutional level, bypassing USD entirely. In practice, this is used for a tiny fraction of bilateral trade. Vision 2030 fintech reforms may open new channels mid-term.
Scenario D
When WorldFirst is NOT the best option
For one-off personal transfers under SAR 5,000, Tahweel Al Rajhi’s SAR 17 flat fee may result in a lower absolute cost. For cash-to-cash emergency transfers, Western Union’s speed (minutes) is unmatched. For institutional oil/petrochemical settlements, Bank of China / ICBC Riyadh provide dedicated infrastructure with potentially negotiated rates that could beat WorldFirst on very large amounts.

What This Analysis Does Not Cover

No Saudi bank publishes CNY-specific FX rates — CNY is not listed as a tradeable pair on any Saudi retail banking platform. All bank costs are estimated from published FX margin ranges for non-USD currencies. The actual SAR→CNY rate may differ from the SAR→USD→CNY chain estimate.

Bank of China / ICBC retail pricing unknown — Both Chinese banks in Saudi Arabia focus on institutional/corporate clients. Retail transfer pricing, if available, is not published. These could be competitive for direct CNY but are not accessible to most SMEs.

Exchange house CNY capability uncertain — Tahweel Al Rajhi and other exchange houses support China as a destination but specific CNY pricing is not published. FX markup at exchange houses may differ significantly from bank rates.

WorldFirst Saudi Arabia registration not confirmed — WorldFirst’s availability for Saudi-based senders is not explicitly confirmed on their website. P0 Longtail pricing is applied as the assumed tier. Actual availability should be verified directly.

Negotiated corporate rates excluded — Large-volume clients at Saudi banks or through Bank of China / ICBC may receive substantially better pricing.

Petro-yuan trajectory uncertain — The speed of CNY adoption for oil settlement could dramatically reshape this corridor. Current analysis reflects April 2026 reality, not future state.
