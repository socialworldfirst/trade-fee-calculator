French bank transfers to China carry an FX markup of approximately 1.2–4% above the EUR/CNY mid-market rate. At the mid-market rate of 7.95 CNY per EUR (3 April 2026), a EUR 10,000 transfer via Société Générale delivers an estimated EUR 238–338 less value than the same transfer via WorldFirst. The exchange rate markup — not the stated wire fee — constitutes approximately 85–95% of total transfer cost across all providers.

EUR/CNY is traded directly on China's Foreign Exchange Trade System (CFETS), making it more liquid than GBP/CNY. This structural advantage contributes to BNP Paribas posting the narrowest markup of the French banks surveyed, at approximately 1.2–2.5%. Despite Paris hosting a designated RMB offshore clearing center (Bank of China Paris, established 2014), the majority of French SMEs continue to pay Chinese suppliers in USD by default, incurring a double conversion with associated cost layers.

Fintech providers cluster at a substantially lower cost level. Wise applies 0% markup plus a transparent fee economically equivalent to approximately 0.41–0.59% on a EUR 10,000 transfer. Airwallex's combined rate of 0.6% (0.5% FX + 0.1% payout) is competitive for B2B use. WorldFirst's 0.40% China payment fee is the lowest corridor-specific charge among all providers researched in this corridor.

§3 — Key Findings

1
Paris is a designated offshore RMB clearing center (Bank of China Paris, established 2014), underpinned by an ECB-PBOC currency swap of CNY 350 billion / EUR 45 billion. Despite this infrastructure, French SMEs overwhelmingly pay Chinese suppliers in USD by default, routing through a double conversion (EUR→USD→CNY) rather than the direct EUR→CNY channel that Paris's clearing architecture enables.
2
BNP Paribas's exchange rate includes a markup of approximately 1.2–2.5%, making it the most competitive French bank in this cohort. Its Chinese banking presence (BNP Paribas China, Bank of Nanjing partnership) contributes to tighter routing efficiency. Société Générale's markup is approximately 2–4%, placing it at the upper end of the range.
3
Banque Populaire/BPCE integrates Wise directly within its mobile application, making it the only major French bank to offer near-mid-market rates natively to its customers. Via this integration, the effective rate is EUR 45–60 on EUR 10,000 rather than the EUR 214–414 that applies through the bank's own international transfer product.
4
Crédit Agricole publishes a 0.05% "commission de change" in its tariff schedule, which understates the true cost of an international transfer. The dealing room spread applied on top of this disclosed commission adds approximately 2–3.5% — a component that is embedded in the quoted exchange rate and not separately itemised for the customer.
5
N26's international transfers are powered by Wise under a white-label arrangement, providing access to the same mid-market exchange rate and fee structure as the Wise standalone product. French N26 customers therefore access Wise's effective rate without a separate Wise account.
6
Airwallex's 0.6% total rate (0.5% FX markup + 0.1% payout fee) is competitive with Wise for business transfers. Its Netherlands-based EU regulatory licence means it operates under the same PSD2 framework as French institutions, providing equivalent EU consumer protections for business clients.
7
WorldFirst's 0.40% China payment fee is the lowest corridor-specific rate among all providers researched for the FR-CNY corridor. Combined with a 0.75% FX ceiling and EUR 0.30 transfer fee, the total cost on EUR 10,000 is approximately EUR 78 — a margin approximately 48% below the cheapest French bank rate (BNP at approximately EUR 150).
8
EUR/CNY is traded directly on CFETS, the Chinese interbank foreign exchange market, with greater liquidity than GBP/CNY or AUD/CNY. This structural liquidity advantage potentially explains the tighter observed bank FX spreads in the France corridor compared with the UK corridor, where GBP/CNY lacks equivalent direct market depth.

§4 — Cost Breakdown by Route

The USD route remains the default for most French SME importers. The buyer converts EUR→USD at their French bank, wires USD via SWIFT to China, and the supplier's Chinese bank converts USD→CNY upon receipt. Three cost layers apply, of which the buyer is typically aware of only the first.

Layer 3 — FX Risk Buffer Embedded in USD Prices (Buyer Pays, Indirectly)

Chinese suppliers embed an estimated 2–5% FX risk buffer into USD-quoted prices to protect against exchange rate movements between quotation and settlement. This cost is structural: invisible on the invoice but present in the unit price. The EUR/USD exchange rate introduces an additional source of uncertainty for French buyers quoting and paying in USD, as EUR/USD movements between order placement and settlement add cost on the buyer's side as well. Paying in CNY directly eliminates the rationale for the buffer on the supplier's side.

Trade finance surveys indicate approximately one in five Chinese exporters add a premium of approximately 10% to USD invoices specifically to cover currency risk. EUR-based buyers using the USD route carry a compounded FX exposure: EUR/USD between order and payment, and the supplier's USD/CNY risk buffer in the invoice price.

§9 — Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to send EUR 10,000 from France to China?
Based on published rates as of April 2026, the cost ranges from approximately EUR 41 (Wise) to EUR 416 (Société Générale) on a EUR 10,000 transfer. Through a French bank, the typical cost falls in the range of EUR 150–416, comprising an FX markup of approximately 1.2–4% above the EUR/CNY mid-market rate (1 EUR = 7.95 CNY), a wire fee of EUR 9–20, and possible correspondent bank charges. WorldFirst's total cost on the same transfer is approximately EUR 78, based on a 0.75% FX ceiling, a EUR 0.30 transfer fee, and a 0.40% China payment charge.
Is BNP Paribas cheaper than Wise for sending money to China?
No. BNP Paribas's exchange rate includes a markup of approximately 1.2–2.5% above the EUR/CNY mid-market rate, plus a EUR 15 wire fee, placing the total cost at approximately EUR 150–265 on EUR 10,000. Wise's effective cost on the same transfer is approximately EUR 41–59. BNP Paribas is the most competitive French bank in this cohort, but remains materially more expensive than specialist fintech providers for EUR-to-CNY transfers at standard retail rates. BNP's corporate markets desk may offer narrower spreads for high-volume clients.
Does Banque Populaire offer cheap transfers to China?
Banque Populaire/BPCE's own international transfer product applies an estimated markup of approximately 2–3%, costing EUR 214–314 on EUR 10,000. However, Banque Populaire integrates Wise directly within its mobile app, offering customers access to Wise's near-mid-market rates (approximately EUR 45–60 effective on EUR 10,000) without needing a separate Wise account. Banque Populaire is the only major French bank to offer this integration. The key practical point is that the cost outcome depends entirely on which pathway the customer uses within the app.
Does Paris being an RMB clearing center make bank transfers cheaper?
Paris was designated as an offshore RMB clearing center in 2014, with Bank of China Paris as the clearing bank, underpinned by an ECB-PBOC currency swap of CNY 350 billion / EUR 45 billion. This infrastructure supports direct EUR-CNY settlement without routing through USD, reducing potential conversion costs at the clearing level. However, the benefits of this infrastructure have not materially translated into lower retail or SME bank FX margins. French SMEs continue to pay Chinese suppliers predominantly in USD by default, and French bank markups for EUR/CNY transfers remain in the range of 1.2–4%.
How long does a bank transfer from France to China take?
French bank transfers to China via SWIFT take approximately 3–5 business days in standard conditions. Processing cut-off times and SWIFT routing paths affect delivery speed; transfers initiated late in the business day may be held until the following day. Specialist fintech providers deliver substantially faster: WorldFirst achieves same-day CNY delivery for approximately 80% of transfers; Airwallex achieves same-day via local rails where eligibility is met; Wise delivers instantly for approximately 74% of CNY transfers via Alipay or WeChat.
What is the cheapest way to send money from France to China?
Based on published rates as of April 2026, Wise has the lowest effective cost at approximately EUR 41–59 on EUR 10,000 (0% markup plus a transparent fee). Airwallex at 0.6% total is the next most cost-efficient option for B2B transfers. WorldFirst at approximately EUR 78 offers the lowest corridor-specific China payment fee (0.40%) of any provider researched, alongside CNY holding, forward contracts, multi-currency infrastructure, and 1688.com payment capability. The lowest-cost option for any specific transfer depends on amount, recipient type, and transfer frequency.
Are fintech providers like Wise and WorldFirst regulated in France?
All providers operating in France are subject to regulation under the EU Payment Services Directive 2 (PSD2) framework. French banks (BNP, SG, CA, BP) are directly authorised by the ACPR (Autorité de Contrôle Prudentiel et de Résolution). EU-licensed EMIs and payment institutions (Wise, Airwallex, Revolut, Remitly) passport into France from their EU home regulator under PSD2. Wise is licensed in Belgium; Airwallex is licensed in the Netherlands (DNB); Revolut holds a Lithuanian banking licence. Customer funds at EMIs are held in safeguarded accounts with major European banks, separated from the firm's own money, but are not covered by French FGDR deposit insurance (which applies only to ACPR-authorised banks to EUR 100,000).
Should French buyers pay Chinese suppliers in CNY or USD?
Paying in CNY directly is the most cost-efficient route when using a specialist fintech provider. It eliminates the supplier's USD→CNY conversion cost (0.3–1.0%), bypasses SWIFT intermediary deductions, and reduces the FX risk buffer that suppliers typically embed in USD-quoted prices (an estimated 2–5%). For French buyers, the USD route introduces an additional layer of EUR/USD exchange rate exposure not present in the CNY direct route: EUR/USD movements between order placement and payment settlement are a cost that falls on the buyer in the USD route. Paris's RMB clearing infrastructure makes EUR-CNY settlement structurally accessible. Survey data from trade finance providers indicates that 36% of Chinese suppliers actively prefer to receive payment in RMB; switching to CNY can result in price reductions of 1–3% on the same goods.

Regulatory Context

France imposes no outbound capital controls on EUR transfers. Transfers above EUR 10,000 are subject to standard AML (Anti-Money Laundering) requirements under the EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives (AMLD4/5/6) as transposed into French law. All providers in this research operate under the ACPR or equivalent EU regulatory passporting framework. China imposes capital controls on CNY inflows. Foreign entities and individuals receiving CNY in China must comply with SAFE (State Administration of Foreign Exchange) reporting requirements above certain thresholds. Businesses receiving USD convert via their commercial bank under SAFE's current account settlement rules. Fintech providers operating local CNY delivery rails manage PRC compliance on behalf of their clients through Chinese banking or Ant Group partnerships.

Paris as an RMB Clearing Centre

Bank of China Paris was designated as the official RMB clearing bank for France and the eurozone in 2014, creating the infrastructure for direct EUR-CNY settlement outside the USD correspondent chain. The ECB-PBOC bilateral currency swap agreement of CNY 350 billion / EUR 45 billion provides liquidity backstop for this clearing function. EUR/CNY is tradeable directly on CFETS, giving it greater onshore liquidity than most non-Asian currency pairs. These structural advantages have not yet produced materially lower retail or SME bank FX margins; the gap between clearing-level efficiency and SME pricing remains wide.

§11 — Methodology and Limitations

What This Analysis Covers

This report covers the cost of sending EUR from France to a CNY-denominated bank account in mainland China, for transfer sizes of EUR 500–50,000. Providers include four major French banks by personal current account market share plus seven fintech and specialist providers active in the France–China corridor as of April 2026.

What This Analysis Does Not Cover

Negotiated corporate rates. Banks offer bespoke FX arrangements for high-volume clients. Businesses transferring above EUR 100,000 per month may receive markups materially below published retail rates. This analysis uses standard published retail/SME online rates throughout.

Forward contract pricing. All costs assume spot-rate transfers at the time of instruction. Forward contracts (available from WorldFirst, Airwallex, OFX, XE, and select banks) carry different pricing structures not modelled here.

Premium and private banking tiers. BNP Paribas Private Banking, Société Générale Private Banking, and equivalent tiers may apply lower FX margins. These are not reflected in this analysis.

EUR–CNY specific markup gap. No French bank publishes a EUR→CNY-specific margin. Data is derived from overall international payment markup ranges and rate comparison testing. CNY may attract an exotic-currency premium above the standard EUR/USD or EUR/major pair rates disclosed in bank tariff schedules.

Receiving bank fees in China. Chinese banks may charge a receiving fee of RMB 0–150 per inbound transfer. These are not modelled as they vary by recipient's bank relationship.

Scope boundary. Import duties, VAT, customs brokerage, trade finance instruments, and supplier credit terms are excluded.

Data Gaps and Limitations

Crédit Agricole's 0.05% stated commission materially understates the actual cost; the dealing room spread component is not separately disclosed and is estimated from rate comparison testing. Revolut pricing is plan-dependent and highly variable across six plan tiers; cost estimates reflect the Standard plan as the baseline. EUR/CNY bank-specific markups are estimated from published tariff ranges and independent rate comparison testing rather than from published per-currency margins.

Data Confidence

Overall confidence rating: HIGH. FX markup data is cross-referenced across 3–5 independent sources per bank including: Wise EU Price Comparison Research, MoneyTransferComparison.com, Finextra, and MoneySavingExpert equivalent French sources. Transfer fees are sourced from bank websites and published tariff schedules. Regulatory status is verified against ACPR public registers and EU passport notification records.
