Pakistani bank transfers to China carry an estimated FX markup of approximately 2–4% above the PKR/USD mid-market rate, compounding further at the China-side USD→CNY conversion. At 1 USD = 279 PKR (3 April 2026), the PKR equivalent of $10,000 is approximately PKR 2,790,000. A Pakistani commercial bank (HBL, MCB, UBL, Allied) processing this transfer via the USD route is estimated to apply a 2–4% FX markup on PKR→USD conversion, plus a flat SWIFT fee of approximately USD 5–6 and a PKR correspondent surcharge of approximately PKR 1,000.

Fintech coverage of this corridor is exceptionally thin. Only OFX appears in Monito's tracked data for Pakistan outbound, at approximately 4.8% effective cost. Wise cannot source PKR: Pakistani users cannot convert PKR and send abroad through Wise's standard platform. JazzCash and Easypaisa, the dominant Pakistani mobile money platforms, are configured for inbound remittances and do not support outbound B2B trade payments.

The World Bank's Remittance Prices Worldwide database reports no qualifying outbound services for Pakistan, describing the market as having insufficient formally-tracked providers to produce a benchmark. This corridor is among the thinnest in the cross-corridor dataset. Bank FX markup figures are derived from independent comparison data and estimated ranges; no Pakistani bank publishes explicit FX margin schedules.

ICBC Pakistan, designated as Pakistan's official RMB clearing bank, offers PKR–RMB direct settlement at an estimated 1–2% cost, but access is restricted to large corporate clients and CPEC-linked enterprises.

§3 — Key Findings

1
The World Bank's Remittance Prices Worldwide database reports "no qualifying services" for Pakistan outbound corridors, reflecting a market too thinly served by formally-tracked providers to produce a benchmark. This is the only corridor in this dataset for which the World Bank reports no data.
2
RMB settlement of Pakistan–China imports rose from 5.6% in FY2016 to 19.1% in FY2025, driven by CPEC infrastructure investment and ICBC's designation as Pakistan's RMB clearing bank. This is among the fastest documented shifts in settlement currency composition in an emerging market corridor.
3
Wise cannot source PKR — Pakistani users cannot convert PKR and send abroad via Wise's standard platform. JazzCash and Easypaisa, which together serve over 50 million registered users in Pakistan, are configured exclusively for inbound remittance receipt and do not support outbound B2B trade payments.
4
Letters of Credit remain the dominant payment instrument for Pakistan–China trade, adding documentation overhead, bank charges, and processing time that simple wire transfers avoid. LC fees typically add 1–2% of transaction value on top of the FX cost, making the all-in cost of the LC route substantially higher than the wire route estimates cited in this report.
5
Pakistan's currency swap with China was expanded to RMB 30 billion in October 2024. The State Bank of Pakistan submitted a request to expand it further to RMB 40 billion in April 2025, signalling continued policy commitment to reducing USD dependency in bilateral settlement.
6
ICBC Pakistan holds the official designation as Pakistan's RMB clearing bank and has stated a strategic objective of making Pakistan "South Asia's RMB hub." It operates three branches (Karachi, Islamabad, Lahore) and is the only provider offering direct PKR–RMB settlement at scale, albeit restricted to corporate clients.
7
Hawala and informal transfer networks are the real price competitor in this corridor, offering faster delivery and lower observed costs than any formally-tracked provider. They are undocumented, unregulated, and illegal for trade payments under SBP rules. WorldFirst competes in the formal market by offering compliance-grade documentation at rates approaching informal channel costs.
8
Pakistan's import-to-export ratio with China is approximately 7:1 ($20.2B imports vs. $2.8B exports in FY2024), creating structural, persistent demand for outbound FX. This imbalance — combined with Pakistan's broader current account pressures — contributes to the scarcity of affordable outbound payment channels.

Layer 3 — FX Risk Buffer Embedded in USD Prices (Buyer Pays, Indirectly)

Chinese suppliers invoicing Pakistani buyers in USD embed an estimated 2–5% FX risk buffer into quoted prices to protect against exchange rate movements between quotation and settlement. This cost is structural and invisible on the invoice. Pakistani buyers transacting in USD are exposed to this buffer in addition to the bank-side conversion markup. Switching to CNY payment eliminates the rationale for the supplier-side buffer.

§8 — Decision Framework

Provider selection in the Pakistan–China corridor depends heavily on company size, CPEC linkage, and whether formal compliance documentation is prioritised over cost alone. The informal market alternative (hawala) represents an additional consideration not relevant in other corridors, but it is illegal for regulated import payments.

Scenario A
SME Importers — Below $50,000/Month
WorldFirst represents the most accessible formal option with digital onboarding and competitive P1 tier pricing. The estimated saving over a commercial bank (approximately $50–150 per $10,000 transfer) is narrower than other corridors but cumulative over annual import volumes. The principal advantage over bank alternatives is the elimination of LC documentation overhead, faster settlement, and the ability to pay 1688.com suppliers directly via World Pay at 1.5%. OFX is the only alternative tracked fintech; its all-in cost (~4.8%) is higher than WorldFirst (~3–4.5%).
Scenario B
Large CPEC-Linked Enterprises
ICBC Pakistan's direct PKR–RMB settlement channel at an estimated 1–2% is the most cost-efficient option in the corridor. Access is restricted to corporate clients with a CPEC project nexus or inter-bank relationship with ICBC. The Bank of China Pakistan branch serves a similar profile. For qualifying enterprises, this route offers the lowest all-in cost and direct CNY settlement, eliminating both the USD intermediate conversion and the supplier-side FX risk buffer.
Scenario C
Freelancers and Service Exporters (Receiving Payments)
Payoneer is the dominant inbound payment platform for Pakistani freelancers receiving USD from international clients. For this profile, Payoneer functions as a receive-and-hold platform, with subsequent conversion to PKR at the 2% FX rate. Wise cannot source PKR for outbound transfers; however, Pakistani freelancers holding USD via Payoneer or other accounts can in some cases use USD-based transfer channels for outbound payments. This is not the standard Pakistan importer profile and is noted for completeness.
Scenario D
1688.com Buyers
WorldFirst's World Pay feature for 1688.com purchases is a unique capability in this corridor, enabling direct platform payments at the 1.5% payment fee. Pakistani importers sourcing from 1688 directly — rather than Alibaba's B2B portal — benefit from this integration. No other tracked provider offers a comparable direct 1688 payment rail. This is the most differentiated WorldFirst feature for the Pakistani small importer segment.
Scenario E — Context Note
Formal Channels vs. Informal Networks
For many Pakistani traders, the real choice is between formal bank/fintech channels and hawala networks that are faster, cheaper, and operationally simpler but undocumented and illegal for regulated import trade payments under SBP rules. WorldFirst competes in the formal market specifically: it offers near-informal pricing (4.5% vs. estimated bank 5%) with SBP-compliant documentation. For businesses that require formal payment records for customs, tax, or CPEC financing purposes, WorldFirst's documentation trail is itself a material advantage beyond the cost comparison.

§9 — Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to send $10,000 from Pakistan to China?
Based on estimated data as of April 2026, sending the PKR equivalent of $10,000 from Pakistan to China through a commercial bank (HBL, MCB, UBL, Allied) costs approximately 4–6% of the transfer value, comprising an estimated FX markup of 2–4% on PKR→USD conversion, a wire fee of approximately USD 5–6, and PKR 1,000 in correspondent charges. Through WorldFirst (P1 tier), the estimated cost is approximately 3–4.5% (1.5% FX ceiling + 1.5% payment fee). These are estimates; no Pakistani bank publishes explicit FX margin schedules, and the World Bank's Remittance Prices database reports no qualifying services for this corridor.
Should I pay my Chinese supplier via Letter of Credit or wire transfer?
Wire transfer carries a lower total cost than a Letter of Credit for most SME trade payments. LC fees typically add 1–2% of transaction value in bank charges (issuance fee, amendment fee, negotiation fee) on top of the FX conversion cost. For established supplier relationships where trust is not in question, a wire transfer via a fintech provider or commercial bank is more cost-efficient. Letters of Credit remain appropriate where the buyer has no prior relationship with the supplier or where trade finance or government project documentation requires a formal LC structure (as is common in CPEC-linked procurement).
Why is RMB settlement growing for Pakistan–China trade?
RMB settlement of Pakistan–China imports grew from 5.6% (FY2016) to 19.1% (FY2025), driven by three factors: CPEC project financing disbursed directly in RMB through ICBC and Bank of China; Pakistan's bilateral currency swap with China (expanded to RMB 30 billion in October 2024); and ICBC Pakistan's designation as the official RMB clearing bank. The State Bank of Pakistan has a stated policy interest in reducing USD dependency in bilateral trade. Corporate entities with CPEC linkages can access PKR–RMB direct settlement through ICBC Pakistan at an estimated 1–2% cost, substantially below the USD route.
Why can't I use Wise to send money from Pakistan to China?
Wise does not support PKR as a source currency for outbound transfers. Pakistani users cannot convert PKR and send abroad via Wise's standard platform as of April 2026. This reflects a combination of SBP regulatory requirements for outbound FX (Form E/EIF, NTN documentation, and SBP approval for certain payment types) and the operational complexity of PKR sourcing for a non-bank fintech. Wise does support inbound transfers to Pakistan (Pakistani freelancers and families can receive USD/EUR/GBP via Wise), but outbound B2B trade payments are not available.
What documents does the SBP require for sending money to China for imports?
The State Bank of Pakistan requires Form E (Export Form) or EIF (Electronic Import Form) for regulated trade payments. An NTN (National Tax Number) is required for business transfers. Advance import payments of up to 100% of invoice value have been permitted since January 2024 (a policy change from the prior 25% cap during Pakistan's 2022–23 currency crisis). SWIFT transfers require standard commercial documents: invoice, packing list, and in some cases a bank certificate of purpose. LC-based payments require additional bank-intermediated documentation. SBP may impose additional scrutiny during periods of FX reserve pressure.
What are the risks of using hawala to pay Chinese suppliers?
Hawala and informal transfer networks are illegal for regulated import trade payments under SBP rules. Using hawala for import payments creates multiple legal exposures: failure to file Form E/EIF, non-compliance with SBP's foreign exchange regulations, and potential AML/CFT liability. In addition to legal risk, hawala transfers carry no formal documentation trail, which creates problems for customs clearance, tax deductions on import costs, and any future audit. For businesses that require compliant payment records for CPEC financing, government contracts, or export credit purposes, the absence of a formal payment trace eliminates eligibility. WorldFirst and regulated banks provide SBP-compliant documentation for all transfers.
What is ICBC Pakistan's role as RMB clearing bank?
ICBC Pakistan has been designated by the State Bank of Pakistan and the People's Bank of China as Pakistan's official RMB clearing bank. This means ICBC Pakistan provides inter-bank RMB clearing services — other Pakistani banks can route RMB settlements through ICBC's system rather than through a third-country correspondent. ICBC operates three branches (Karachi, Islamabad, Lahore) and has stated a strategic objective of positioning Pakistan as "South Asia's RMB hub." In practice, the PKR–RMB direct settlement channel is accessible primarily to large corporate clients and CPEC-linked enterprises; SME importers cannot access ICBC Pakistan's clearing rates directly.
What is the cheapest formal way to pay a Chinese supplier from Pakistan?
For corporate clients with CPEC linkage or inter-bank access, ICBC Pakistan's direct PKR–RMB settlement at an estimated 1–2% is the lowest-cost formal option in the corridor. For standard SME importers, WorldFirst (P1 tier, approximately 3–4.5%) is the most cost-efficient tracked fintech option, ahead of OFX (approximately 4.8%) and commercial banks (estimated 4–6%). The narrowness of the bank-to-WorldFirst differential (approximately 0.5%) means the primary WorldFirst advantage in this corridor is digital access, compliance documentation, and 1688 World Pay integration, rather than cost differential alone. Data collected April 2026; no Pakistani bank publishes explicit FX schedules.

CPEC and Bilateral Trade Context

China–Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) committed approximately $65 billion across power, infrastructure, and industrial zones. Phase 2, focused on industrialisation and agricultural modernisation, is underway. CPEC financing disbursements in RMB create direct demand for PKR–RMB settlement that bypasses the USD route entirely. The bilateral currency swap (RMB 30 billion, October 2024 — expansion to RMB 40 billion requested April 2025) supports SBP's FX reserve management and enables CNY-denominated trade credit.

Pakistan's trade deficit with China is substantial: approximately $20.2 billion in imports vs. $2.8 billion in exports (FY2024), a ratio of approximately 7:1. This structural imbalance creates persistent outbound FX demand that Pakistani commercial banks and the SBP must manage carefully, contributing to both the scarcity of affordable formal payment channels and the persistence of informal transfer networks.

SBP Regulatory Framework

The State Bank of Pakistan regulates all outbound foreign exchange transfers. Key requirements for import payments include: Form E/EIF submission, NTN verification, and purpose documentation. Advance payments up to 100% of invoice value have been permitted since January 2024, reversing a restrictive cap imposed during Pakistan's 2022–23 currency crisis. During periods of acute FX reserve pressure, SBP has historically imposed additional restrictions on import payments, contributing to delays and diversion to informal channels.

The SBP interbank FX rate and the open market rate maintain an observed spread of approximately 0.3–0.5%, which is relatively stable post-2023 crisis. Commercial banks are required to offer rates within a defined corridor of the SBP interbank rate, but enforcement of the corridor creates incentives for fee structuring that may not be reflected in published documentation.

Interbank vs. Open Market Spread

The interbank PKR/USD rate and the open market rate have historically diverged significantly during PKR depreciation episodes. The post-2023 stabilisation narrowed the spread to approximately 0.3–0.5%. For businesses transacting at commercial bank rates, the effective cost reflects the bank's markup above the SBP interbank mid-rate — not the open market rate. The two are currently close; historical episodes have seen open market premiums of 10–30% above the interbank rate during crisis periods.

§11 — Methodology and Limitations

What This Analysis Covers

This report covers the estimated cost of sending PKR from Pakistan to a CNY-denominated bank account in mainland China, for transfer sizes equivalent to USD 500–50,000. Providers include the four major Pakistani commercial banks, two Chinese state banks operating in Pakistan, and three fintech/specialist providers with documented or estimated Pakistan outbound capability as of April 2026.

What This Analysis Does Not Cover

World Bank benchmark data. The World Bank's Remittance Prices Worldwide database reports "no qualifying services" for Pakistan outbound corridors. No formal benchmark exists for this corridor from the World Bank's standard dataset. This is the only corridor in this cross-corridor study for which World Bank data is unavailable.

Bank FX markup schedules. No Pakistani commercial bank publishes an explicit FX margin schedule for outbound international transfers. All bank-side markup estimates in this report are derived from SBP FX monitoring data, Monito comparison tracking, and cross-corridor benchmarks. Confidence for bank-side data is LOW.

Hawala and informal channel pricing. Informal transfer networks are used extensively in this corridor and may offer lower observed costs than formal channels. They cannot be formally documented, are illegal for import trade payments under SBP regulations, and are excluded from this analysis.

ICBC Pakistan corporate rates. ICBC Pakistan's direct PKR–RMB clearing rates for corporate clients are not published and were not accessible for this research. The 1–2% estimate is derived from general corporate RMB clearing benchmarks and may not reflect actual ICBC rates.

Letter of Credit costs. LC-based trade finance is the dominant payment instrument for Pakistan–China trade but adds 1–2% in bank fees above the FX transfer cost. LC economics are not modelled in detail in this report.

SBP advance payment restrictions. The current 100% advance payment allowance (January 2024) may be subject to future SBP policy changes during periods of FX reserve stress. Historic restrictions have materially affected the corridor's formal payment capacity.

Negotiated corporate rates. Large Pakistani importers (above $1M/month) may negotiate FX rates with commercial banks below the estimated retail markup ranges. This analysis uses standard retail/SME rates throughout.
