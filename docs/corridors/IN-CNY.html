03 — Key Findings 8 findings • research grade
  • 01 WorldFirst's P1 Longtail pricing (up to 3% FX + 3% payment fee) is not cost-competitive with Indian banks. SBI offers FX markups as low as 0.125–2% above mid-market, with wire fees of ₹500–2,000. Even HDFC at its worst (3–3.5% FX) plus fees comes in around 4–5% total — comparable to or below WF's P1 rate. This is an honest assessment: in this corridor, the cost argument does not favor WorldFirst. The value proposition rests on platform convenience, speed, and 1688 integration.
  • 02 India and China have no bilateral currency swap agreement despite $155.6 billion in bilateral trade. The PBOC has active swaps with 40+ central banks globally, but political tensions since the 2020 Galwan Valley clash have prevented any RBI–PBOC arrangement. This absence forces all trade settlement through USD, adding double-conversion cost. Direct INR–CNY liquidity is essentially nonexistent.
  • 03 India’s trade deficit with China hit a record $116 billion in 2025. Total bilateral trade reached $155.6 billion (+12% YoY). India imports electronics ($38B), machinery ($26B), and chemicals from China; exports raw materials like iron ore ($1.9B), mineral fuels ($1.3B), and organic chemicals ($1.3B). The payment demand overwhelmingly flows India → China, creating massive outbound payment volume.
  • 04 Political tensions create a unique overlay on this corridor. India banned 300+ Chinese apps in 2020 (TikTok, WeChat, etc.). Press Note 3 imposed mandatory government approval for all FDI from neighboring countries (targeting China). March 2026 partial relaxation allows Chinese investment in electronics, capital goods, and solar — but with Indian majority ownership and 60-day review. China's FDI in India: just 0.32% of cumulative total ($2.51B since 2000).
  • 05 FEMA regulations require all FX to route through Authorized Dealer (AD) banks. The Foreign Exchange Management Act (1999) mandates that all cross-border payments must flow through RBI-licensed AD banks. Private entities cannot hold or transfer foreign currency independently. Import payments require supporting documentation (invoice, Bill of Entry). This regulatory structure limits fintech disruption — even Wise must partner with AD banks for outbound transfers.
  • 06 LRS cap of $250,000 per financial year applies to individual remitters. The Liberalised Remittance Scheme allows Indian residents to remit up to $250,000 (April–March). From April 2025, TCS of 5% applies on remittances exceeding ₹10 lakh per financial year (raised from ₹7 lakh). Business trade payments for documented imports are generally exempt from TCS, but the regime adds compliance overhead and cash flow friction for SMEs.
  • 07 Wise has strong India presence but cannot serve this corridor effectively. Wise received RBI PA-CB in-principle approval in June 2025, but has paused outward remittance onboarding for new users. Existing users can send INR abroad, but Wise does not offer direct INR→CNY conversion. Indian users must send USD and rely on recipient bank conversion. This makes Wise a non-factor for B2B India→China trade payments.
  • 08 The October 2024 LAC disengagement agreement has not materially changed financial infrastructure. India and China agreed to patrolling arrangements at Depsang and Demchok, but both countries maintain aggressive military buildups. India relaxed FDI norms in March 2026 but the broader financial relationship — no currency swap, no direct payment infrastructure, no bilateral fintech cooperation — remains frozen. The payment corridor infrastructure lags far behind the $155.6B trade reality.

08 — Decision Framework By buyer segment
SME Importers (<$50k/month)
SBI or ICICI Bank
For standard trade payments to Chinese suppliers, Indian banks are 1.5–2.5% cheaper than WorldFirst at P1 pricing. Use your existing bank relationship. The SWIFT delay (2–5 days) is an acceptable trade-off for the cost saving at this volume.
1688.com Buyers
WorldFirst 1688 World Pay
No Indian bank offers direct 1688 checkout. Without WF, you need a sourcing agent (5–10% commission) or manual SWIFT to the supplier's personal bank account. WF's ~6% total cost is expensive but still cheaper than agent fees, and the integration removes reconciliation friction.
Large Importers (>$100k/month)
SBI (primary) + WorldFirst (supplement)
SBI's negotiated FX rates (0.125–0.5% for large volumes) with full L/C and trade finance capability should be the primary channel. Use WorldFirst as a supplement for urgent same-day payments and 1688 transactions where speed matters more than cost.
Personal / Small Transfers
Wise (if existing user) or Instarem
For personal remittances under $10k, Wise or Instarem at ~1–2% total cost are the cheapest options. Wise's onboarding freeze limits new users. Both lack direct CNY but work for USD-intermediated transfers at significantly lower cost than banks or WorldFirst.
09 — Frequently Asked Questions 8 questions • India-specific
Can Wise send money from India to China? +
Wise has paused outward remittance onboarding from India for new users while reconfiguring under its RBI PA-CB licence (in-principle approval June 2025). Existing users with prior outbound transfer history can still send INR abroad, but Wise does not support direct INR-to-CNY conversion. Indian users must send USD and rely on the Chinese recipient's bank for USD-to-CNY conversion, which adds an uncontrolled markup. At approximately 1.0–1.5% FX on the INR-to-USD leg, Wise is competitive for personal transfers — but the onboarding freeze and absence of direct CNY support limit its practical utility for new business users in this corridor.
What is the RBI Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS)? +
The LRS allows Indian residents to remit up to $250,000 per financial year (April–March) for permitted purposes including trade payments, education, travel, and investment. All outbound remittances must route through an Authorized Dealer (AD) bank. From April 2025, Tax Collected at Source (TCS) of 5% applies on remittances exceeding ₹10 lakh per financial year (raised from the previous ₹7 lakh threshold). TCS is refundable against income tax liability but creates a working capital drag. Business trade payments for documented imports are generally exempt from TCS when routed through proper AD bank channels with supporting documentation.
Is there an India-China bilateral currency swap? +
No. The PBOC maintains bilateral currency swap agreements with over 40 central banks globally — including most major emerging market economies — but there is no active RBI–PBOC currency swap. This absence reflects the deterioration of India-China relations since the 2020 Galwan Valley military clash. Without a swap line, Indian banks lack institutional CNY liquidity. The practical result: all India-China trade settlement routes through USD, with double conversion (INR→USD→CNY) as the structural default.
What are India's restrictions on Chinese business? +
Since 2020, India has imposed escalating restrictions: ban on 300+ Chinese apps (TikTok, WeChat, Alibaba UC), mandatory government approval for all FDI from countries sharing a land border with India (Press Note 3 of 2020, targeting China), enhanced security scrutiny of Chinese entities in telecom and critical infrastructure, and restrictions on Chinese companies bidding for government contracts. In March 2026, India partially relaxed FDI norms for Chinese investment in electronics, capital goods, and solar cell sectors — but with Indian-majority ownership requirements and 60-day processing timelines. The broader framework of suspicion remains. China's cumulative FDI in India is just $2.51 billion since 2000 (0.32% of total).
Why is double conversion (INR to USD to CNY) the standard? +
Three factors converge: (1) No RBI-PBOC currency swap means Indian banks have no institutional CNY liquidity; (2) The INR-CNY pair has negligible direct market depth — there is no meaningful offshore or onshore INR-CNY trading market; (3) Political tensions prevent the bilateral financial infrastructure development that would be expected between the world's 1st and 5th largest economies. The practical result is that banks quote INR-to-USD and USD-to-CNY as two separate transactions, each with independent FX markups. Combined spreads of 2–4% are typical through Indian banks.
What does FEMA require for outbound trade payments? +
The Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA, 1999) requires all cross-border payments to route through RBI-licensed Authorized Dealer (AD) banks. Import payments must be supported by documentation: commercial invoice, Bill of Entry, import contract, and IEC (Import Export Code). Payments must align with contracted terms. AD banks are authorized to permit extensions beyond agreed due dates. Private entities cannot independently hold or transfer foreign currency — all FX must route through the AD banking system. This framework limits fintech disruption, as even digital platforms like Wise must partner with AD banks for outbound transfers from India.
What is TCS on foreign remittance and does it apply to trade payments? +
Tax Collected at Source (TCS) is collected by banks and remittance providers on outward transfers. From April 2025: 5% TCS on remittances exceeding ₹10 lakh per financial year for most purposes (investment, travel, etc.); 20% TCS for investments and property purchases above ₹10 lakh. TCS is refundable as a credit against income tax. For bona fide business trade payments (imports with supporting documentation routed through AD banks), TCS exemption generally applies. However, the exemption requires proper documentation and varies by AD bank interpretation — some banks apply TCS on all outbound transfers and require the taxpayer to claim refund at filing. This creates cash flow friction for SME importers.
How does WorldFirst's 1688 World Pay work for Indian buyers? +
WorldFirst's 1688 World Pay enables direct checkout on 1688.com (Alibaba's domestic wholesale marketplace) from a WorldFirst account. For India at P1 Longtail pricing, the estimated cost is approximately 3% FX + 3% platform fee = ~6%. This is expensive compared to other corridors. However, no Indian bank offers direct 1688 checkout integration. Without 1688 World Pay, Indian buyers must use a sourcing agent (typically 5–10% commission), pay via Alipay/WeChat through unofficial channels, or arrange manual SWIFT transfers requiring suppliers to reconcile payments with orders. The platform integration value is operational efficiency and supplier relationship management, not FX savings.

The India-China corridor is defined by a paradox: $155.6 billion in bilateral trade operating through financial infrastructure designed for adversaries. India and China are the world's two most populous countries, the 5th and 2nd largest economies, and share a 3,488 km disputed border — yet they have no bilateral currency swap, no direct payment rail, and no fintech cooperation. Every rupee sent to China must route through USD, adding structural cost.

India's managed float regime means the RBI actively intervenes in FX markets. The rupee has weakened from ~83 to ~93 against USD over the past year, making imports from China progressively more expensive. The RBI uses the USD as its primary intervention instrument — buying or selling dollars to smooth excessive volatility — but does not target any specific exchange rate level.

The political overlay is inescapable. The 2020 Galwan Valley clash killed 20 Indian soldiers and led to app bans, FDI restrictions, and a freeze on bilateral financial infrastructure. The October 2024 LAC disengagement agreement and March 2026 FDI relaxation signal a thaw — but the financial infrastructure remains frozen in 2020. For cross-border payments, the practical effect is: more friction, more cost, fewer options than the trade volume would justify.

India's trade composition with China is structurally asymmetric: India exports raw materials (iron ore, naphtha, shrimp, castor oil) and imports high-value manufactured goods (electronics at $38B, machinery at $26B, chemicals, plastics). This means payment demand is overwhelmingly India→China, and Indian importers bear the cost of the underdeveloped payment infrastructure.

11 — Data Limitations Methodology notes • Next review: October 2026
  • Indian bank FX markups are estimated ranges based on published indicative rates, third-party analysis (MoneyHOP, Karboncard, Wise blog), and market intelligence. Banks do not publicly disclose exact FX margin schedules — actual spreads are negotiated per client relationship, transaction size, and corridor.
  • WorldFirst P1 Longtail tier pricing ("up to 3% FX" and "3% payment fee") represents maximum published rates. Actual rates for specific accounts may be lower based on volume, account tier, and negotiation. We have used the published maximums for conservative comparison.
  • Wise and Instarem INR-to-China cost estimates assume USD-intermediated transfers, not direct CNY. Neither provider confirmed direct INR→CNY routing. The recipient-side USD→CNY conversion adds an uncontrolled variable cost not included in our Wise/Instarem estimates.
  • TCS treatment of trade payments varies by AD bank interpretation. Some banks exempt documented imports; others collect TCS and require taxpayer to claim refund. Our analysis assumes trade payments are TCS-exempt where properly documented, but this should be confirmed with the specific AD bank.
  • Instarem and BookMyForex's China B2B trade payment capability is unconfirmed. Both platforms appear primarily focused on personal remittance. Their inclusion reflects availability for personal transfers, not confirmed B2B trade support.
  • Payoneer's India-China capability is payout-directional (seller receives). We classified it as "not available" for buyer-initiated trade payments. Payoneer's Easylink acquisition may expand capabilities in future.
  • The $155.6 billion bilateral trade figure is calendar year 2025 (China customs data). India's financial year data (FY25, April 2024–March 2025) shows $127.71 billion. Difference reflects methodological and calendar differences, not an error.
  • Data collected April 2026. Exchange rates, regulatory requirements, and provider pricing are subject to change. Next scheduled review: October 2026.
