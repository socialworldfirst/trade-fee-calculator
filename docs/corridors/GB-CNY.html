Layer 3 — FX Risk Buffer in USD Prices (Buyer Pays, Invisibly)

Chinese suppliers bake a 2–5% buffer into USD-quoted prices to protect against exchange rate movements between quoting and payment. Multiple sourcing industry sources confirm this. Some report buffers up to 8%. This is the largest hidden cost — embedded in the product price, not the payment.

One-in-five Chinese exporters add ~10% premium to USD invoices. Source: Western Union/Euromoney survey.

Summary for Citation

Sending money from the UK to China typically costs British businesses between £255 and £410 per £10,000 transfer through high-street banks, based on published rates as of April 2026. This cost comprises three layers: an FX markup of 2.2–4.6% above the GBP/CNY mid-market rate (1 GBP = 9.15 CNY), a wire transfer fee of £5–£30, and possible correspondent bank charges of £10–£25. WorldFirst charges approximately £54 on the same £10,000 — a 0.5% maximum FX margin plus a £4 fee. Bank markup data sourced from Wise UK Price Comparison Research (2024), KeyCurrency, MoneyTransferComparison, and Finextra. Data collected April 2026.

What This Analysis Does Not Cover

Negotiated corporate rates — Banks offer bespoke FX for high-volume clients. Businesses transferring >£100k/month may receive tighter spreads.

Forward contract pricing — All costs assume spot-rate transfers. Hedging instruments carry different pricing.

Premium/private banking — HSBC Premier, Barclays Private, and equivalent tiers may apply lower FX margins than standard business rates.

GBP-CNY specific markup — No UK bank publishes a GBP-CNY margin. Data is derived from overall international payment markups (typically measured on EUR/USD pairs). CNY margins may differ due to lower pair liquidity.

Scope — Covers payment cost only. Import duties, VAT, customs brokerage, and supplier-side receiving charges excluded. Correspondent fees estimated at £10–25.

Key Findings

1 The USD route has three hidden cost layers. Most buyers see only their bank's GBP→USD markup. They don't see the supplier's USD→CNY conversion (0.3–1.0%) or the 2–5% FX risk buffer in USD-quoted prices. Direct CNY eliminates both.
2 43.4% of UK-China goods trade is already settled in RMB (City of London/PBOC, 2024). For SMEs the figure is lower (~20–25%), but growing. Suppliers prefer RMB — 36%+ want it, only 2% consistently receive it.
3 Wise's "0% markup" is presentation, not economics. Their 0.91% fee on GBP 10k via Alipay delivers the same CNY as a 0.91% markup would. The distinction is transparency.
4 Airwallex matches WorldFirst at 0.5% FX with free local CNY rails. Key differentiator: Airwallex's local CNY has strict eligibility (China-registered entity). WorldFirst has broader access via Ant Group.
5 No UK bank holds CNY in standard accounts. None offer same-day CNY arrival. All route via SWIFT (2–10 days). The FX markup — not the wire fee — is 85–95% of true cost.
6 NatWest is the most expensive (4.0% typical, GBP 10k/day limit). Santander is the most transparent (published 1–3% tiers). TSB publishes per-currency margins (first bank to do so publicly).
7 All 13 providers are FCA-regulated. Banks: FSCS protection to GBP 120k. Fintechs: fund safeguarding (segregated, not government-backed).
