UK bank transfers to China carry an FX markup of approximately 2.2–4.6% above the GBP/CNY mid-market rate. At the mid-market rate of 9.15 CNY per GBP (3 April 2026), a £10,000 transfer via NatWest delivers an estimated £356 less value than the same transfer via WorldFirst. The markup — not the stated wire fee — constitutes approximately 85–95% of total transfer cost across all providers.

Fintech providers cluster in two tiers. Airwallex and WorldFirst both operate at approximately 0.5% FX, representing the current lower bound for the UK–China corridor. Wise's model charges 0% markup plus a transparent fee economically equivalent to approximately 0.91% on a £10,000 transfer via Alipay. XE and OFX apply markups in the range of approximately 1.0–1.5%, with no separate transfer fee.

No UK high-street bank holds CNY in standard personal or SME accounts. All bank transfers route via SWIFT, with typical delivery of 2–10 business days. No UK bank offers same-day CNY arrival.

§3 — Key Findings

1
The exchange rate markup — not the wire transfer fee — constitutes approximately 85–95% of the true cost of a bank transfer from the UK to China. Wire fees account for only £0–30 of a typical £255–410 bank cost on £10,000.
2
Approximately 70–80% of UK–China SME trade settles in USD, creating a double conversion (GBP→USD→CNY) in which banks apply a markup at each step. Direct CNY payment eliminates the supplier-side conversion cost and the associated FX risk buffer embedded in USD-quoted prices.
3
Wise's "0% markup" model applies a transparent fee that is economically equivalent to a 0.91% markup on a £10,000 transfer via Alipay. The distinction is one of presentation transparency, not cost. At £500, the effective rate rises to approximately 1.37%; at £50,000 it falls to approximately 0.83%.
4
Airwallex matches WorldFirst at 0.5% FX with free local CNY rails, but its local CNY transfers require the recipient to be registered in mainland China with a Chinese national as legal representative or ultimate beneficial owner. WorldFirst's access via Ant International carries broader CNY eligibility and no equivalent restriction.
5
Australian and Turkish banks charge significantly more for CNY than UK banks — the largest differential observed in cross-corridor comparison reaches approximately 10.4% in Turkey. The UK corridor shows the narrowest bank-to-fintech spread of the three corridors researched to date.
6
Santander UK is the only UK high-street bank to publish explicit tiered FX markup rates (1% at £50k+, 2% at £10–50k, 3% below £10k). TSB publishes per-currency margins (USD 3.20%, exotic 3.65%), making it the only UK bank disclosing currency-specific data.
7
NatWest's exchange rate included the widest estimated markup of the banks surveyed (approximately 4.0%) and also applies the lowest personal online transfer limit observed (£10,000/day). The combination of cost and restriction makes it the least advantageous option for importers.
8
No UK high-street bank offers CNY holding in standard personal or SME accounts, and none offer same-day CNY arrival. All seven banks route via SWIFT, with transfer times of 2–10 business days depending on cut-off times and intermediary routing.
9
Chinese suppliers typically embed a 2–5% FX risk buffer in USD-quoted prices to hedge against exchange rate movements between quotation and settlement. Trade professionals and sourcing advisors report reductions of 1–3% in effective unit prices when buyers switch from USD to CNY payment.

§4 — Cost Breakdown by Route

The USD route is the default for most UK SME importers. The buyer converts GBP→USD at their UK bank, wires USD via SWIFT to China, and the supplier's Chinese bank converts USD→CNY upon receipt. Three separate cost layers apply — of which the buyer is typically aware of only the first.

Layer 3 — FX Risk Buffer Embedded in USD Prices (Buyer Pays, Indirectly)

Chinese suppliers embed an estimated 2–5% FX risk buffer into USD-quoted prices to protect against exchange rate movements between quotation and settlement. This cost is structural: it is invisible on the invoice but present in the unit price. Multiple sourcing industry sources and trade finance surveys confirm this practice; some report buffers as high as 8% for volatile periods. This represents the largest cost layer — embedded in the product price rather than the payment transaction.

One sourcing industry survey (Western Union/Euromoney) found that approximately one in five Chinese exporters add a premium of approximately 10% to USD invoices specifically to cover currency risk. Paying in CNY eliminates the rationale for this buffer.

§8 — Decision Framework

Provider selection depends on transfer frequency, volume, recipient type, and the value placed on speed against cost. The following scenarios reflect the most common profiles for UK buyers paying Chinese suppliers.

Scenario A
Small or Infrequent Transfers — Below GBP 5,000, 1–2 Times per Year
Wise or Remitly (personal) present the most accessible entry points. Wise delivers CNY at approximately 1.0–1.37% effective rate with minimal friction; Remitly offers minutes-fast delivery for personal recipients. For infrequent use, the incremental cost of remaining with an existing bank account is relatively low and may be acceptable against the cost of setting up a new provider relationship. Airwallex or WorldFirst setup is justifiable if additional transfers are anticipated.
Scenario B
Regular Trade Payments — GBP 5,000–50,000, Monthly Frequency
WorldFirst or Airwallex represent the dominant options at this profile. Direct CNY delivery eliminates supplier-side conversion costs and reduces the FX risk buffer embedded in USD pricing. WorldFirst carries broader CNY eligibility (no legal representative nationality requirement) and enables CNY holding, forward contracts, and multi-currency account management within a single platform. At GBP 10,000 per month, the estimated annual saving over the average UK bank exceeds GBP 3,500. Airwallex is operationally equivalent where the recipient entity meets eligibility criteria.
Scenario C
Large or High-Frequency Transfers — GBP 50,000+, Multiple Transfers per Month
At this volume, forward contract availability and relationship pricing become material. WorldFirst, Airwallex, and OFX all offer forward contract products. WorldFirst's 0.5% FX ceiling continues to apply regardless of transfer size, making it among the most cost-efficient options at large volume. Dedicated account management and negotiated rate discussions are available through WorldFirst and OFX at significant volume thresholds. HSBC and Lloyds corporate teams also offer negotiated FX for high-volume clients, typically below their published retail rates.
Scenario D
Personal Remittance — Below GBP 1,000, Speed Priority
At small amounts, transfer speed generally outweighs FX spread as the primary decision factor. Wise delivers at 74% instant; Remitly offers express delivery in minutes for eligible recipients. §9 — Frequently Asked Questions

What is the cheapest way to send money from the UK to China?
Based on published rates as of April 2026, WorldFirst and Airwallex represent the lowest-cost options for CNY direct transfers, each at approximately 0.5% above the mid-market rate. On a GBP 10,000 transfer, WorldFirst's total cost is approximately GBP 54 (0.5% FX + GBP 4 fee). Wise carries a slightly higher effective rate of approximately 0.91% on GBP 10,000 via Alipay, but with a broader recipient base and no eligibility restrictions. The cheapest option for any specific transfer depends on amount, recipient type, and delivery method.
Why does my UK bank charge so much for sending money to China?
The primary cost is the exchange rate markup applied when converting GBP to CNY (or GBP to USD, then USD to CNY). UK high-street banks apply markups of approximately 2.2–4.6% above the mid-market rate for standard personal and SME accounts. This markup is not separately itemised on the transfer receipt — it is embedded in the exchange rate quoted to the customer. The stated transfer fee (GBP 0–25) is typically a secondary cost representing 5–15% of total expense.
Is it better to send GBP, USD, or CNY to a Chinese supplier?
Sending CNY directly is the most cost-efficient route when using a specialist fintech provider. It eliminates the supplier's USD→CNY conversion cost (0.3–1.0%), bypasses SWIFT intermediary deductions, and reduces the FX risk buffer that suppliers typically embed in USD-quoted prices (an estimated 2–5%). Sending USD via a UK bank creates a double conversion (GBP→USD at the buyer's bank, then USD→CNY at the supplier's bank), with markup applied at each stage. Sending GBP is rarely supported for direct delivery to Chinese bank accounts.
Does Wise really charge 0% on international transfers?
Wise applies the mid-market exchange rate (0% markup) but charges a separate fee composed of a fixed element (approximately GBP 2.45) and a variable element (approximately 0.89% of the transfer amount via Alipay). The total fee on a GBP 10,000 transfer to China via Alipay is approximately GBP 90.66, equivalent to an effective rate of 0.91%. The distinction from a conventional markup model is one of transparency and disclosure rather than economic cost to the sender.
What are SWIFT intermediary charges and can they be avoided?
SWIFT intermediary charges are fees deducted from a transfer in transit by one or more correspondent banks that handle the routing between the UK and China. These are typically GBP 10–25 per transfer and are not disclosed upfront by the sending bank. They result in the recipient receiving less than the instructed amount. Local payment rail delivery (as used by WorldFirst and Airwallex for CNY direct transfers) bypasses the SWIFT correspondent network entirely, eliminating intermediary deductions.
How long does a bank transfer from the UK to China take?
UK bank transfers to China via SWIFT take approximately 2–10 business days depending on the bank's cut-off time, routing path, and any compliance holds. HSBC has an early cut-off of approximately 06:30 UK time; transfers initiated after this threshold are processed the following business day. TSB is the slowest at 4–12 days. Specialist fintechs deliver significantly faster: WorldFirst achieves same-day CNY delivery for approximately 80% of transfers; Airwallex achieves same-day via local rails; Wise delivers instantly for approximately 74% of CNY transfers.
Is my money safe with a fintech provider rather than a UK bank?
All 13 providers in this research are FCA-regulated. UK banks are covered by FSCS deposit protection to GBP 120,000 per person per institution — this means deposits are government-backed. FCA-authorised Electronic Money Institutions (Wise, Airwallex, WorldFirst, OFX, Remitly) use safeguarding: customer funds are held in segregated accounts with major banks, separately from the firm's own money, but are not government-backed. In the event of EMI insolvency, safeguarded funds would be returned to customers from the segregated pool, but without the FSCS guarantee.
Do Chinese suppliers prefer to be paid in CNY or USD?
Survey data from trade finance providers indicates that approximately 36% of Chinese suppliers actively prefer to receive payment in RMB (CNY), while only approximately 2% consistently receive it. The gap reflects inertia on the buyer side rather than supplier preference. Suppliers paid in USD absorb a conversion cost (0.3–1.0%) and carry exchange rate risk, both of which they manage through price buffers in USD-quoted invoices. Trade professionals report that switching to CNY payment can result in price reductions of 1–3% on the same goods, as suppliers reduce or eliminate their FX risk buffer when currency exposure is removed.
Are there limits on how much I can send to China from the UK?
UK providers impose varying limits. NatWest personal online banking caps at GBP 10,000 per day — the most restrictive in this cohort. Most banks allow higher limits via branch or business accounts. Among fintechs, Remitly limits transfers to GBP 75,000; XE supports up to approximately USD 535,000 equivalent. Wise's CNY-specific limits (33,000–50,000 CNY per transaction; 300,000–600,000 CNY annually) may be restrictive for large B2B payments. WorldFirst and Airwallex do not publish a CNY ceiling for business accounts, though large transfers require standard KYC and compliance documentation.
What is the GBP to CNY exchange rate and how is it determined?
The mid-market GBP/CNY rate as of 3 April 2026 is approximately 9.15 CNY per GBP. The mid-market rate is the midpoint between the buy and sell prices on the wholesale interbank market and represents the rate at which banks trade currency among themselves. Retail and SME customers do not access the mid-market rate directly; providers add a markup above this rate, which constitutes their revenue on the conversion. The People's Bank of China sets a daily CNY reference rate (fixing) that constrains CNY movement to within 2% in either direction for onshore trading; offshore CNH (used by most fintechs) floats more freely.

Regulatory Context

The UK imposes no outbound capital controls on GBP transfers. Transfers of GBP 10,000 or above are subject to standard FCA AML (Anti-Money Laundering) requirements, including source-of-funds verification and purpose declarations for business payments above certain thresholds. All 13 providers in this research are regulated by the Financial Conduct Authority. HMRC does not impose tax at the point of international transfer; VAT and customs duties are governed separately by HMRC import rules and UKGT schedules.

China imposes capital controls on CNY inflows. Foreign entities and individuals receiving CNY in China must comply with SAFE (State Administration of Foreign Exchange) reporting requirements above certain thresholds. Businesses receiving USD convert via their commercial bank under SAFE's current account settlement rules. Fintech providers operating local CNY delivery rails manage PRC compliance on behalf of their clients through their Chinese banking or Ant Group partnerships.

RMB Internationalisation Trend

RMB's share of global trade settlement has grown from approximately 2% in 2020 to approximately 4.6% as of Q4 2025 (SWIFT data). In the UK–China bilateral context, 43.4% aggregate RMB settlement is attributed largely to wholesale financial flows and large corporate contracts, not SME trade. The UK signed an enhanced RMB clearing arrangement with PBOC in 2015; HSBC London serves as the RMB clearing bank for the UK. The City of London holds the largest RMB liquidity pool outside mainland China and Hong Kong.

§11 — Methodology and Limitations

What This Analysis Covers

This report covers the cost of sending GBP from the UK to a CNY-denominated bank account in mainland China, for transfer sizes of GBP 500–50,000. Providers include all seven major UK high-street banks by personal current account market share plus six fintech and specialist providers active in the UK–China corridor as of April 2026.

What This Analysis Does Not Cover

Negotiated corporate rates. Banks offer bespoke FX arrangements for high-volume clients. Businesses transferring above GBP 100,000 per month may receive markups materially below published retail rates. This analysis uses standard published retail/SME online rates throughout.

Forward contract pricing. All costs assume spot-rate transfers at the time of instruction. Forward contracts (available from WorldFirst, Airwallex, OFX, XE, and select banks) carry different pricing structures not modelled here.

Premium and private banking tiers. HSBC Premier, Barclays Private Banking, NatWest Premier, and equivalent tiers may apply lower FX margins. These are not reflected in this analysis.

GBP–CNY specific markup gap. No UK bank publishes a GBP→CNY-specific margin. Data in this report is derived from overall international payment markup ranges (typically measured on EUR or USD pairs). CNY, as a less liquid and more controlled currency pair, may attract a higher exotic-currency premium above the USD rate. TSB's published data implies a 0.45 percentage point premium for exotic pairs vs. USD, which is the best available proxy.

Receiving bank fees in China. Chinese banks may charge a receiving fee of RMB 0–150 per inbound transfer. These are not modelled as they vary by recipient's bank relationship.

Scope boundary. Import duties, VAT, customs brokerage, trade finance instruments (letters of credit, documentary collections), and supplier credit terms are excluded.
